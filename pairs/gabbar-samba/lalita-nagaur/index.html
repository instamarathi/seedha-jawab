Contractor साहब ऊँची जात के हैं, साहब, हमारे गाँव में उनकी बात उठाना ख़तरे की बात है. मैं panchayat office दो बार गई — pradhan जी ने कहा "अगले हफ़्ते आना, file देख लेंगे." अगला हफ़्ता आता है, अगला हफ़्ता जाता है. मेरा बेटा कल रात बोला — "माँ, आज रोटी पे क्या है?" कुछ नहीं था. साहब, मैं कहाँ जाऊँ?

Summary

Lalita, 27, is a daily-wage MGNREGA labourer in a Nagaur village in Rajasthan, working on gravel-road repair. She has a valid job card, an Aadhaar-linked Jan Dhan account, and roughly sixteen days of attendance correctly marked on the muster roll for each of the past three months — November, December, January. Her account still shows zero. At ₹266 per day, three months of work is ₹12,768 — the money she counts on for her two small children's milk and school fees while her husband works in Jaipur. The contractor has offered three different excuses on three visits: "system slow", "Aadhaar mismatch", "computer error". The Bank Mitra has confirmed in person that no government transfer has arrived in her account — meaning the payment was blocked before it reached her, not after. Another woman from the same worksite, Reshma, has the same problem. The panchayat pradhan has told her twice to "come back next week". She cannot speak openly in the village — the contractor is from a dominant caste, she is not. Gabbar names the design plainly: this is not a system error, this is fund diversion under Section 25 of the MGNREGA Act, and the panchayat channel is compromised so the fight must move to Block and District level, on paper, in parallel. The plan is four complaints filed in the same week so no single officer can sit on the file. One: get a paper or photo copy of the muster roll from the worksite supervisor — every MGNREGA worker has the legal right to her own attendance record, and this is the strongest piece of evidence. Two: call the NREGA helpline 1800-345-22-44, free, Hindi, generating a complaint number, and within the same week walk into the Nagaur Block Development Officer's office with Reshma to file a written complaint under MGNREGA Section 25 with date-stamped acknowledgment. Three: a written demand for a Social Audit of the worksite — mandated by law every six months and almost certainly overdue — submitted at both the Gram Sabha and the Block office. Four: an online Banking Ombudsman complaint at bankingombudsman.rbi.org.in asking the bank itself to confirm where the transfer is — a thirty-day legal response window. If any caste-based threat or intimidation follows, Women's Helpline 181 is open twenty-four hours, and the SC/ST (Prevention of Atrocities) Act 1989 makes such intimidation non-bailable. The most important shift is from one complainant to two: Lalita and Reshma together. One file can be buried; two files with two signatures and two muster-roll copies cannot. The shame is not Lalita's. It is the system's. She is not asking for charity; she is asking for sixteen days a month of work she has already done.
