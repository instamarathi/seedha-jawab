Pranam. मेरा naam है Rajan Tiwari, उम्र 52, Gorakhpur से लिख रहा हूँ — Bichhiya, near the Cantt area. पिछले फ़रवरी में retire हुआ हूँ — Indian Railways से, 30 साल की सेवा. Senior Section Engineer (Signal & Telecom), North Eastern Railway. Last posting Gonda-Mankapur section में थी. आपको शायद पता न हो — Railway के signal systems पूरी दुनिया में सबसे complex में से हैं. Interlocking, route-setting, axle counters, track circuits, panels — एक misfire और 1500 लोगों की train दूसरी train पे ठोक सकती है. हम वो आदमी हैं जो रात के 3 बजे जब siren बजती है तो उठ के Section में दौड़ते हैं, ₹40 की लालटेन ले के — साहब, आज भी कुछ junctions पे लालटेन ले के signals check करते हैं, foggy December morning में. यह कोई office job नहीं था — यह जान का काम था. अब मेरा बेटा — Devansh, 26, IIT Roorkee से B.Tech (Electrical), अब Pune में एक product company में Senior Software Engineer है — Lithium ion battery management systems पे काम करता है. Package मेरे retirement से double है, मुझे पता है, भले ही उसने कभी मुझे बताया नहीं. पिछले Holi पे घर आया था, बात हो रही थी, मैंने कहा — "बेटा, तेरा काम तो एक तरह से deterministic है ना — code लिखा, test किया, deploy किया. हमारा काम field में था — हर section unique, हर weather अलग, हर station master अलग, हर एक decision real-time. Holi के बाद मैंने ख़ुद बैठ के एक list बनाई — 30 साल में जो problems मैंने solve किए — एक बार Bareilly junction पे कोहरे में जो interlocking failure हुई थी, मैंने अकेले 4 घंटे में 18 panels का sequence reset किया, बिना manual के, क्योंकि manual लाने वाला आदमी bike से रास्ते में फँसा हुआ था. यह story मेरे बेटे को नहीं पता. वो AWS deployment की बात करता है, मैं सुनता रहता हूँ. साहब, मेरी request straight है — मेरे बेटे को कोई बताए कि उसका papa भी एक creative आदमी था. और कि signal engineering "deterministic" नहीं है — वो engineering का सबसे creative form है, क्योंकि वहाँ हर decision की क़ीमत में 1500 ज़िंदगियाँ हैं. यह बात मेरे मुँह से निकले तो लगती है boasting, पर सच है. आप जो भी पढ़ते हो — बेटे को convince कीजिए. मेरी 30 साल की मेहनत को कम मत समझा कीजिए, अपने papa को.

Rancho: Rajan ji, सबसे पहले एक बात साफ़ करता हूँ — आप ग़लत नहीं हैं signalling के बारे में. Summary

Rajan Tiwari, 52, retired in February as a Senior Section Engineer (Signal & Telecom) on the North Eastern Railway after thirty years on lines from Gonda to Mankapur, including a much-told incident in which he reset eighteen panels at Bareilly junction in four hours of fog without a manual. His son Devansh, 26, is a software engineer in Pune working on Lithium-ion battery management systems. At Holi, Rajan opened a comparison: signalling, he said, was real-time creative work, while software was deterministic. Devansh laughed and brushed it off — "Papa, modern software is more complex than you imagine." It cut. Rajan now wants the column to write to his son and tell him signalling was the more creative work. Rancho first does what Rajan asked for: he confirms, on technical merit, that railway signalling is one of engineering's most underappreciated state-machine disciplines, with failure costs in human lives. Rajan is not boasting; he is correct. But Farhan reads the letter for what it is. The request to "tell my son he is wrong" is not the real request. The real request is to be seen — by the only person whose seeing now matters, his son. Devansh did not disrespect his father; a 26-year-old engineer building his own identity in Pune simply could not yet recognise the older creature standing across the dining table. The advice goes against the asking. Do nothing toward Devansh directly. Instead, two practices for Rajan himself. One, buy a notebook and write the thirty years out — Bareilly first, then everything before and after, one story a week, fifty stories in a year. Indian Railways is automating; men like him will be rare in twenty years; this archive is public good before it is filial. When Devansh next comes home, the notebook stays open on the table; if curiosity arrives, it arrives without a sermon. Two, take genuine interest in the son's work. Ask one specific question about thermal runaway and cell-to-cell propagation in BMS systems; an old signal engineer has the basics to follow the answer 70% of the way. Devansh will see his father is still an engineer, and the comparison will dissolve without anyone winning it. Farhan closes with his own ache. His doctor father told 36-hour-shift stories he laughed off; the stories were never the point, attention was. He never gave it. The pattern repeats across generations and cannot be broken in one conversation. But it can be softened — by writing the stories down, so that when the son's moment comes, decades from now, the father's voice is still there to be heard. Stop trying to win the signalling argument. Thirty years of Indian Railways is witness enough. The son is not a critic; he is a son. He will arrive, in his own time.
