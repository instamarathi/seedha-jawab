Munna: Rahul, सुनिए. आपने जो लिखा है — 'मैं उस घर के लिए तरस रहा हूँ जो अब exist नहीं करता' — यह sentence आपके पूरे letter का सच है, और इस feeling का नाम भी है, और आपको बताना ज़रूरी है कि यह feeling एक bug नहीं है, यह feature है. यह feeling हर उस आदमी के अंदर आती है जो अपने घर से दूर एक अच्छी ज़िंदगी बनाता है, और जिसके माँ-बाप पीछे रहते हैं — यह नौकरी नहीं है, यह geography का एक side-effect है. और इस feeling को pathologize मत कीजिए — यह आपके connected होने की निशानी है. एक disconnected आदमी हर महीने ₹85,000 नहीं भेजता, हर दो हफ़्ते video call नहीं करता, December में 12 दिन के लिए घर नहीं जाता. आप connected हैं — बस आप दो time-zones में connected हैं. और एक last बात. आप 27 के हैं. यह feeling — homesickness for a home that no longer exists — यह आपकी पूरी ज़िंदगी रहेगी, हल्की हल्की. यह जाएगी नहीं, इसका कोई 'cure' नहीं है — पर यह बढ़ेगी भी नहीं अगर आप इसको नाम दे दें और इसे feel करने की permission ख़ुद को दें. रात को Al Barsha के flat में spreadsheet के बाद थोड़ी देर के लिए Thrissur को याद करना — यह time waste नहीं है, यह आपको आदमी बनाए रखता है. Spreadsheet वाला आदमी अकेले नहीं चल पाएगा — उसको HC Verma वाले 18 साल के Rahul के साथ-साथ चलना है, हमेशा. वो आदमी मरा नहीं है — वो आपके अंदर है, बस अब उसकी role बदल गयी है. वो अब पहले वाला driver नहीं है, अब वो co-passenger है. यह बहुत क़ीमती co-passenger है — इसे रखिए, बस steering wheel से थोड़ा हटाइए.

Circuit: "Rahul भाई, एक छोटी बात Munna भाई के बाद. आप जो ₹85,000 भेजते हैं हर महीने 48 महीने से बिना miss किए — यह सिर्फ़ पैसे नहीं हैं, यह एक sentence है हर महीने माँ-बाप को — 'मैं हूँ, मैं ज़िंदा हूँ, मैं याद रखता हूँ.' यह बहुत बड़ा काम है, यार. आप ख़ुद को judge मत कीजिए कि 'feeling नहीं हो रही' या 'गलत feeling हो रही' — feelings वो होती ही हैं जो होती हैं. और एक बात — Velayudhan chettan को आप नहीं जानते, ठीक है, पर माँ जानती हैं, और माँ बात कर रही हैं तो आप माँ की बात सुन रहे हैं. यही असली connection है. हम Munna भाई की तरह philosophical नहीं हैं, पर इतना समझते हैं — अगर आदमी हर 14 दिन में अपनी माँ का video call attend कर रहा है, तो वो अकेला नहीं है, और वो लोग भी अकेले नहीं हैं. यह बात आप ख़ुद को बार-बार बोलिए जब Al Barsha में रात को coffee पीते हुए ceiling पे fan देखते हो."

Summary

Rahul Nair, 27, is a software engineer in Dubai, originally from Thrissur, Kerala. He works at a mid-size fintech in a backend role, lives alone in a 1BHK in Al Barsha, and has sent ₹85,000 home to his parents every month for forty-eight months without a single miss. He writes that nothing is wrong — his job, salary, visa, and health are all fine — yet he carries a feeling he cannot name. Every two weeks his mother holds the phone for a video call while his father sits behind on the sofa with the news running low; she updates him on neighbours — Velayudhan chettan's heart attack, Sreedevi aunty's daughter's engagement, Pillai sir's retirement, the new principal Mariamma teacher. Rahul has no memory of any of these people. He says "haan Amma, accha" through every update because telling the truth would hurt her, and her ability to speak about him to those neighbours depends on his pretending to remember them. When he visited home for twelve days in December, he found his old room exactly as he had left it in March 2016 at the end of Class 12 — same Sachin and Messi posters, same NASA poster, same blue Onida fan, his HC Verma physics textbook still bookmarked at the page where he had stopped reading rotational motion ten years ago. He writes that the room is a museum of an older version of himself, that he is homesick not for a home but for a home that no longer exists, and asks Munna and Circuit what this feeling is called. Munna names it precisely. The feeling is not a bug; it is a feature of being connected across two timelines that have not aged together. Thrissur's clock has moved forward; Rahul's mental Thrissur is frozen in 2016. The parents have kept the room frozen as a shrine where they meet their eighteen-year-old son; Rahul has kept the parents frozen as the people he saw the day he left. Both sides love each other, but each is loving a slightly outdated version of the other. The diagnosis is right — and the prescription is small. Two changes, no drama. First, stop saying "haan Amma, accha" to the neighbour updates. Next time, gently ask: "Amma, mujhe poora yaad nahi hai unka — kaun se waale the, mere time pe kya karte the?" Mothers love giving detail; she will be happy, and slowly she will absorb that her son is now twenty-seven and not eighteen, without any painful confrontation. Second, do not disturb the museum room — for the parents that room is a sacred way of missing him, and disturbing it would hurt them. Instead, every annual visit, add one small object from his Dubai life — a book, a souvenir, a photograph from outside Al Barsha — to the room. One object a year. In ten years, ten objects. The room becomes a dialogue across decades instead of a frozen moment, and his current self begins to enter the parents' awareness through the dust they wipe off these new things. Circuit closes by reminding Rahul that the eighty-five thousand rupees every fortnight for four years is itself a sentence — "I am here, I remember, I am alive" — and that the act of attending the call, listening to news of Velayudhan chettan whose face he cannot place, is itself the connection, not a failure of it. The feeling will not leave him; that is not what is asked of it. It is asked only to be named, allowed, and given a smaller seat in the car of his life — co-passenger, not driver.
