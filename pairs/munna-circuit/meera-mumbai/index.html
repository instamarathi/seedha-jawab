Summary

Meera is a 23-year-old junior data scientist at a large consumer-products company in Mumbai, ten months into her first job. She is an IIT Kharagpur mechanical engineer from Nashik — top of her school, the achiever every relative in her parents' building knows about. She is also the only junior on her eight-person data science team who comes to the BKC office every single day. Her two cohort-mates — equally credentialed, joined the same week — work from Bengaluru and Hyderabad offices, mostly remote. On Friday evening her first appraisal review delivered a slightly-below-average rating of 2.7, against a team average of 3.0; her two WFH peers received 3.1 and 3.2, the "on track" bucket. They shared their numbers in the juniors' WhatsApp group on Saturday morning with celebration emojis. Meera has not shared hers. To compound the wound, the manager who delivered the rating ended the conversation by suggesting — kindly, almost as a postscript — that she should "think about an MBA at some point; some folks really find their stride after one." She is writing the letter from her bed in a Sakinaka 1BHK, flatmates out for Sunday brunch, LinkedIn open with the "Open to Work" toggle hovering. Her question is not really whether to resign — she knows that math. It is whether the feeling she has is real, or whether she is being defensive because, for the first time in her conscious life, she has been called below average. Munna and Circuit name the structural shape. The rating she received was set by the outgoing head — an eleven-year company veteran whose final appraisal cycle this was — and is therefore a closed verdict on signed paper that cannot be argued with now; the judge has left the chamber. But the lever is the new head: six weeks in, no inherited opinion of her, an entirely empty page on which her next rating gets written over the coming quarter. Their advice unfolds in three parts. First, on Monday morning, send two short emails: one to the manager asking for two or three concrete examples behind the rating and a written follow-up; one — much shorter — to the new head, simply introducing herself and offering to share her work in a 20-minute conversation. Second, don't resign at ten months. The career math is brutal — first-job exits before eighteen months read on recruiter screens as "couldn't survive first job," and the next role gives the same legibility problem with a weaker hand. The job to leave is the next one, with a year of experience and a story she wrote herself. The MBA decision is in the same category — it is a five-year choice, not a Friday-evening one. Third, three concrete, personality-preserving legibility moves: after every meeting, Slack-summarise your own point in writing before it can be re-attributed; take two WFH days a week to reduce the "drowning" surface area horizontal teams see; and build one coffee-mentor in each horizontal team over a quarter so the 360 inputs themselves start shifting. Circuit closes by naming the truth of the letter: Meera is not weak, she is not defensive, she is a sharp engineer with a single legibility gap — and "unmissable" is not the same word as "loud." There is a door open on the other side that she has not noticed yet because she is still staring at the closed one. Sunday's only task is sleep. Monday's task is two emails.
