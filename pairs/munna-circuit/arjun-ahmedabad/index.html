Summary

Arjun, 43, runs a textile business in Ahmedabad inherited from his father and roughly doubled in twenty years. He has built a four-bedroom house in Bodakdev, sent two children to a good school, and provided two cars, a driver, a cook, jewellery, land. His wife Hetal is gentle, capable, and has run the household entirely on her own for twenty years. Arjun comes home every evening and feels, at his own dinner table, like a polite guest. Nobody is ignoring him. Decisions get made and reported to him. He cannot find words for it. He once tried to say it; Hetal laughed and said "you provide everything, we are yours." That wasn't the point. Munna names the difference: providing is not presence. Twenty years of being the respected, busy father has produced a working family system in which Arjun's role is mostly to keep the system funded — and the system, by long natural drift, has stopped routing day-to-day life through him. This is nobody's fault. It just happened. Three things, all hard, none expensive. First, sit Hetal down one evening and tell her, plainly, that he feels in the house but not inside it — she is exhausted too, and probably has no language for it either; that twenty-minute conversation could be the most important of the marriage. Second, put one office hour, five evenings a week, on lockdown — a real hour, with cricket, dance class recordings, real questions. Third, take ownership of one household task fully — parent-teacher meetings, dance recital tickets, cricket kit shopping. Important fathers are feared. Available fathers are approached. Twenty years of drift will not unwind in a week, but the direction reverses the day Arjun stops paying for the party from outside the gate and walks in.
