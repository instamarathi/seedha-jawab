Summary

Hema, 34, is a senior product manager in Bengaluru — seven years at a US SaaS company, an Exceeds Expectations rating this morning, a comfortable bonus arriving by email. She is also paying off the home loan, the parents' medical insurance, and managing a four-year-old while her husband runs his own startup in Goa. In her appraisal her manager called her "extremely reliable, the best execution muscle on the team." In the same meeting he mentioned that Karan — a colleague she onboarded two years ago, taught the product roadmap framework, and handed three of her biggest customer relationships to during her maternity leave — has just been promoted to Director, "such a natural leader, we all saw it from day one." She is writing this from the seventh-floor office bathroom, third stall, twenty-two minutes in. She wonders if she is overreacting. Munna and Circuit name the pattern: "reliable" is not a compliment, it is a category — the language of bonuses; "natural leader" is the language of chairs. The gap between the two phrases, applied to two people doing comparable work, is documented in research from Stanford to McKinsey, and it is the most common career-blocker for capable women in corporate India. The advice is in three parts. First, get out of the bathroom and that night, build a clean spreadsheet of the last twelve months — every deal closed, feature shipped, customer retained, person mentored — with numbers. This is not a diary. It is a business case. Then send the manager a two-line email asking what specifically would need to be demonstrated to be considered for the next Director-level opening, and request a six-month written plan with concrete milestones. The email pins him and starts a paper trail. Second, fix the visibility problem — Karan was not promoted because he was smarter; he was promoted because he was in the rooms where decisions get made. Volunteer to present at the next town hall, post weekly about her work on LinkedIn, and cold-message a senior woman PM outside the company for a 30-minute coffee mentorship. Third, open a backup track simultaneously — update the resume, take two recruiter calls in the next month, learn the market price for a Senior PM in Bengaluru. A confirmed offer in hand changes the conversation inside the current company by 100%. Circuit closes by naming what the question really shows: at home she is the unspoken CEO of her family — loan, insurance, parents, child — because at home there is no one to call her reliable instead of leader. The same competence is hidden at work because she has been quiet about it. Quiet leadership is a strength at home and a fatal weakness at the office. Monday she walks out, not as the old version of herself, but as someone who has decided to stop translating her own work for other people's vocabulary.
