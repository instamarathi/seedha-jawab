अब question. क्या यह adulthood का natural shape है? क्या मैं emotional हो रहा हूँ अनावश्यक? क्या मुझे accept कर लेना चाहिए कि friendships die — कुछ in stages, कुछ silently? या यह "quiet abandonment" है — वो modern term जो मैंने पढ़ी थी कहीं — जहाँ लोग actively नहीं कहते "तू मुझे ज़रूरी नहीं है" पर behaviour में दिखाते हैं? मैं 36 का हूँ, बेटा 18 month का है, बहुत-कुछ ज़िंदगी में अच्छा है — यह कोई crisis नहीं है, मैं spiral नहीं कर रहा. पर रात में, Aarav के सोने के बाद, balcony में coffee पी के मैं Block C Room 412 के बारे में सोचता हूँ. और एक specific सवाल मुझे खाता है — Sumeer से 8.4 km पे एक आदमी है जो मेरा भाई था. मैं उसको "तू मुझे miss करता है क्या" पूछूँ — यह कैसा सवाल है adult आदमी का? पर अगर न पूछूँ, तो मैं क्या कर रहा हूँ — silent abandonment को normalize?

Arjun, 36, product manager at a Bengaluru fintech, married, with an eighteen-month-old son, has three closest friends from NIT Trichy 2010 — Block C Room 412. Karthik is in Seattle, twelve years a Microsoft engineer, with whom Arjun has three or four video calls a year and one annual in-person meeting in Chennai during Karthik's Diwali visit home. Vikram quit L&T in 2018 and runs a 47-goat organic dairy farm in a village near Nagpur called Kalmeshwar; he can't leave the goats and Arjun has not made it there in seven years despite repeated invitations. Sumeer is a Senior Director at Flipkart, lives 8.4 kilometres away in HSR Layout, and they have met twice in 2025 — both brief, his phone ringing through both. Arjun has initiated three of the last attempts; Sumeer has initiated none. The question is whether this is adulthood's natural shape or what he calls "quiet abandonment." Akash refuses to treat the three friendships as one. Karthik is the healthiest case and needs no fixing — three-or-four-calls plus annual Chennai meet is the working rhythm of an adult immigrant friendship; the only upgrade is a recurring calendar invite, set tonight, for a quarterly call. Vikram is solvable and Arjun's failure to fix it is on him; book a four-day December trip to Kalmeshwar — Aarav at twenty-four months is perfect age, the goats become a real childhood memory, and Vikram finally gets the seven-year-old "next holiday me aa" answered in person. The trip is also a hard reset on Arjun's own Indiranagar treadmill. Sumeer is the painful one because the distance isn't physical — it's intentional. Eight months at 8.4 kilometres while meeting fifty other Bengaluru contacts at networking events is not "busy"; it is priority. The fix is not confrontation. The fix is one final, structured, low-effort, specific invitation: "Sunday 4pm, my flat, Riya and Aarav are home, just chai, thirty minutes." If he comes, the friendship resumes; if he evades and re-evades, Arjun has his answer without ever having to ask the cruel question, and the answer is that 2009 and 2026 are different countries and Sumeer has chosen one. Sameer adds that Arjun's instinct to apologise for missing his friends — "everything else is good, this isn't a crisis" — is itself the problem. Missing friends is not selfish; it is the soul-layer of an adult life and it deserves attention, not dismissal. Block C Room 412 is memory now, but the man who climbed a tree for five hours to get Sumeer's calculator down is still alive and needs new rooms with new roommates: a colleague, a neighbour, a Cubbon Park walk with Aarav and someone willing to walk alongside. Replacement doesn't exist; new connection does.
