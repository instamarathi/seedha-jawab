Summary

Nandini, 29, NGO program manager in Dehradun, has been best friends with Tanya since the fourth grade — same school, same college, same heartbreaks, same kitchen chai. Tanya is marrying Aryan in Mussoorie in December and Nandini is the maid of honor. The complication: three years ago, on a Goa terrace surrounded by common friends, Nandini and Aryan briefly kissed. The next morning Aryan named Tanya as the person he was actually thinking about. Six months later they began dating; in 2024 they got engaged. Nandini never told Tanya, and has been carrying the kiss like a piece of glass for three years. Now she has to give a toast at the engagement, dance to "tum hi ho" with Aryan at the sangeet, and stand in every photograph. Akash and Sameer split the question cleanly. Sameer's first instinct is operatic horror — "YOU CAN'T BE THE MAID OF HONOR!" — but he comes around. Akash's verdict: do not tell Tanya. A confession that exists to redistribute the confessor's guilt is not honesty, it's burden-shifting. Telling Tanya a month before the wedding would cloud the most important day of her life and give her nothing useful in return. Nandini did the right thing three years ago; continue. Do not step down from maid of honor either — she did not betray Tanya (she did not know about Tanya and Aryan when the kiss happened), stepping down would require a lie that is more dangerous than the kiss. The real work is internal: that small grief from three years ago is real, not a crime, and needs to be processed cleanly — with a therapist, in a journal, with a non-overlapping friend — so it lives in some other room when she stands at the mandap. Be the maid of honor properly, write the warm toast, learn the dance steps, be fully present for Tanya. Old best friendship sometimes means carrying something silent — not to hurt, but to protect the person you love. Sameer adds that on the wedding day, when something inside her chinks for a moment, that's normal — breathe, look at Tanya, return to the present. Set the glass down on a side table; her own story at 29 is just beginning.
