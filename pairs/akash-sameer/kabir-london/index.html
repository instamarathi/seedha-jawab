Summary

Kabir, 39, Doon School Class of 2003, has lived in London since 2011 — Lloyds senior manager, married, eight-year-old son, the full upper-middle expat life. His old school WhatsApp group — 31 men, very active, 50-60 messages a day — has slowly become a feed of mutual fund updates, uniform-Monday photos of children, and political forwards. Fifteen years ago this same group debated philosophy at three in the morning. Vikram recited Lagaan dialogues; Rahul started "is there life after death" threads that ran for hours. Today Vikram forwards startup funding announcements; Rahul is an MD at Edelweiss and shares LinkedIn screenshots. Kabir opens the chat every day, types "yaar, remember when—" and deletes it. He wonders whether his friendships have died, whether he is unrealistically expecting people to still be 17, or whether being an immigrant froze time on his side and not on theirs. Akash's answer is precise. The friendships have not died — they have changed shape. Seventeen-year-old friendship is a vibe; thirty-nine-year-old friendship is a trust. The 31-man group is currently doing the trust layer correctly — if anyone falls ill tomorrow, they will all show up. But you cannot get the old vibe back inside a 31-person group; group physics forbids it. So stop looking for it there. Instead, treat the group as a bulletin board, and move the deeper work into one-on-one DMs. Tonight, message Rahul directly — one specific tiny private memory, no "let's catch up sometime" (dead phrase). Mr. Joshi's clerk in a previous life. The night Rahul started life-after-death and you all stayed up. Specific memory is a key; performance scripts dissolve when two people are alone in a chat. He may reply that night, in three days, in a week, with a long voice-note — but he will reply. Sameer makes it operational: twelve such messages over twelve months, one a month, one different friend each time. Half will reconnect, half won't, and that's fine — the cycle of type-and-delete breaks. The distance is real, not denial; tell your eight-year-old son the stories so they live somewhere. And finally: Lloyds senior-manager skills don't help here. This is seventeen-year-old Kabir's job. Give him a chance.
