Summary

Ruksana, 13, lives in Bhopal's Arera Colony and studies in Class 8 at St. Mary's Convent School. Her teacher Mrs. Saxena assigned a group social science project on "Water Crisis in India: A Multi-State Analysis." Mrs. Saxena chose the groups herself, and Ruksana ended up with the three weakest students in the class — Karan who sleeps through every period, Tanya who got 23 marks in History last term, and Vivek who didn't show up for the first four days because his phone was broken. Over nine days Ruksana built a thirty-slide PowerPoint. State-wise water data sourced from the Central Water Commission's 2023 report. Rainfall trends 2010-2024 plotted in Excel. Four slides per state — Maharashtra, Rajasthan, Tamil Nadu, Madhya Pradesh — covering geography, current state, crisis points, and solutions. Comparative analysis. Citations. Consistent fonts. On submission morning at 10:50 Karan walked in with a single A4 sheet — a hand-drawn pie chart of India's water usage by sector. He had written Agriculture 45%, Industry 30%, Domestic 25%. The actual figures are roughly Agriculture 80%, Industry 6%, Domestic 9% — he had simply guessed. Tanya stood behind the projector and contributed nothing. Vivek arrived late and read one of Ruksana's slides aloud. Mrs. Saxena clapped at Karan's pie chart, called it creative hand-made effort, and at the end of the presentation praised the entire group equally — "wonderful collaborative effort." Ruksana cried all night. She didn't tell her mother because she anticipated the standard "life is like this, beta" response. She wants an honest answer: is this how the world works, and if so, should she just do less? Teja and Gogo treat the situation with full Bond-villain gravitas — Karan's wrong pie chart is criminal misinformation, Mrs. Saxena is a system to be navigated, the entire affair is a battlefield for tomorrow's leaders. Underneath the bombast they tell her the actual truth. This isn't injustice exactly, it is the default. Group projects produce group credit everywhere — school, office, politics, film. Mrs. Saxena isn't evil, she is operating in default mode and cannot track every group's internal dynamics across forty-seven students. Ruksana cannot fix the system, but she can build her own inside it. Three concrete actions, starting tonight. One: a personal Google Drive folder for every project, with all source files, drafts, screenshots, and date-stamps preserved as her permanent archive.
