Hello. I am writing this letter on behalf of Madam Veena, age 8, of Bandra West. I am her au pair, Maria, and Madam has dictated the question to me. She has insisted I write everything she says, exactly as she says it. (Madam wishes to add — "and put my full name, Veena Adhiraj-Khanna, with the hyphen.") Right. Here we go. — "I am Veena Adhiraj-Khanna and I live in our family apartment in Bandra. We have an apartment that takes the whole floor — there are two lifts, one is for guests and one is for staff. My father makes movies, my mother does interiors. On 14 April it was my eighth birthday. I had a party. I sent fifty invitations — Maria printed them on the gold paper. I wrote each name myself in my best cursive, even Sahib aunty's name which is very long. The party was a Versailles theme — Maria, write that I picked the theme, not Mom. Mom only approved. Cake was a four-tier from Le15 — pastel pink, gold leaf, my name in icing on top — and we had a chocolate fountain and a real pony, who was named Buttercup but I renamed her Marie Antoinette for the day. I wore a blue dress that came from Paris and shoes that matched. (Madam wishes to add — "the shoes had little crystals — please write that.") Right." — Madam, the actual question? — "Maria, I am coming to it." — "Of fifty invitations, forty-seven people came. Three did not come. Three. The three who did not come are: Aanya Khurana, who lives in our same building two floors below, who sent a message saying she had a fever; Kabir Singhania, who had to go to a wedding in Jaipur, his mother sent flowers and apologised; and Tara Mehrotra, who sent a message at 11 a.m. on the day of the party saying her grandmother had passed away in the morning. THREE invitations were wasted. I had pre-selected gift bags for each of them — Aanya's was a Smiggle stationery set in lavender, Kabir's was a Lego architecture set (the one of the Eiffel Tower), and Tara's was a charm bracelet from the Tiffany kids' line that Maria had to specifically order from Singapore three weeks ago. THREE gift bags went unopened. Maria had to put them away in the storage room. Maria was instructed not to give those gifts to other children at the party because that is unfair to the gifts." — (Madam, do you wish to include the part about the cake?) — "Yes Maria, include the part about the cake. Three slices of cake had to be thrown away because of the absences. The slices had been pre-cut, with the names piped on. They could not be re-piped. The slices that said 'Aanya', 'Kabir' and 'Tara' had to go to the bin. Mom said I could keep them as 'a memory' but Maria said it was unhygienic." — Madam, the question please? — "Right, Maria, you are being aggressive today. The question — to Mr. Teja and Mr. Gogo — is this: How does a person such as me handle people who waste invitations? Is there a way to ban them from future parties? Should I unfollow them on my mother's Instagram, where she posts pictures of me? Should I write them a letter expressing my disappointment? I am thinking of preparing a list — a Black List — and putting their three names on it. The next time my parents host any function — even an adult function — I would like to ensure that these three families do not appear on the guest list. My father says I am being 'a bit much' but he is now in Goa for shooting and Mom says she will support whatever I decide so long as I 'process my feelings.' I do not feel like processing. I feel like banning. — (Maria, please make sure you write — "It is the principle of the matter, not the gifts." — yes, write that.) — Right. So that is the question. Please reply at your earliest. I have another party in June for which the planning starts next month and the same children are likely to be invited."

— Veena, Bandra (typed by Maria, au pair)

Summary

Veena Adhiraj-Khanna, 8, of Bandra West, has had her au pair Maria type out a letter on her behalf complaining that three out of fifty children invited to her birthday party did not attend. Her father makes movies, her mother does interiors; their apartment occupies a full floor in Bandra, with one lift for guests and another for staff. The party was a Versailles theme, the cake a four-tier Le15 with gold leaf, the entertainment a real pony renamed Marie Antoinette for the day. The three absentees: Aanya Khurana, who lives two floors below in the same building and had a fever; Kabir Singhania, who was at a family wedding in Jaipur and whose mother sent flowers; and Tara Mehrotra, whose grandmother passed away that very morning at eleven a.m. Three pre-selected gift bags went unopened — a Smiggle stationery set in lavender, a Lego architecture Eiffel Tower, and a Tiffany kids' charm bracelet ordered three weeks ahead from Singapore — and three slices of cake with names piped on were thrown away. Veena wishes to construct a Black List on which the three families would feature, to be cross-referenced against future guest lists including her parents' adult functions. Her father, currently in Goa for a shoot, has called this "a bit much"; her mother is supportive of "processing your feelings." Veena does not feel like processing; she feels like banning. Teja and Crime Master Gogo treat the matter with the gravitas of a national emergency for the first half of the conversation, with Gogo gleefully comparing details to Mogambo's family functions, before the comedy steps quietly aside. The diagnosis is gentle but precise: there has been no rejection here. One had a fever. One was at a wedding. One lost a grandmother on the morning of the party — and Tara, on Veena's birthday, in another Bandra apartment, was an eight-year-old who would be living without her grandmother for the rest of her life. The Black List instinct is misdirected; what really hurts is not the three who declined but, very quietly, that the forty-seven who came likely did not actually see Veena — they took photos with the pony, ate cake, posted to Instagram, and left, and not one of them sat in front of her without a phone for a full hour and asked what she was reading. The decline-rate of three out of fifty is statistical normality, the kind any adult event sees too. The advice from Teja is structured and tender. First — Tara: do not put her on a list, write her a small hand-written card today: "Tara, I heard about your grandmother. I am with you." Maria can help; this is the gift that will outlast Tiffany. Second — Aanya and Kabir: the politest possible declines, accompanied by flowers and apologies, do not belong on any list anywhere; they belong in the everyday fabric of how a generous adult learns the world works. Third — the forty-seven: the question worth asking is not "who skipped?" but "who in the forty-seven actually saw me?" If even one such friend exists, find her, spend time with her; if none, the problem is not the three absent but the forty-seven who came and did not see, and that has to be looked at with kindness, not punishment. Fourth — Black Lists: a person with a Black List walks into adulthood with a long, lonely list, because once "wasted my invitation" becomes a frame, every dropped phone call and missed Diwali begins to fit it; Mogambo himself, Gogo confides, was kept his Black List of two hundred names and at the end only Gogo came to the hospital. Fifth — and most important — what Veena most needs is not a guest-list audit but one hour with her mother, alone, no phone, no Maria, no Instagram. She must ask for it directly, and her mother must give it. That is the gift she can give herself for her eighth birthday, more meaningful than any party. Gogo's closing word borrows Mogambo's only sound advice — those who came, count them; those who did not come, did not stay away because of you, they stayed away because of their own life, and that distinction, learned at eight, halves the troubles of an adult life. Send Tara a card. Walk down two floors and visit Aanya. Tell Kabir wait until next birthday. One Saturday afternoon, one hour. That, Veena, is the actual party — and the lesson it teaches is the only invitation that is never wasted.
