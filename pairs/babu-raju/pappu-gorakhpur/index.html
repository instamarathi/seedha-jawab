Summary

Pappu Yadav, 47, has run "Pappu chai ka thela" at Mohaddipur chowk in Gorakhpur for twenty-two years — chai with a famous double-anda omelette specialty, regulars including a former SP, BRD Medical College students, and railway colony families. His wife Sunita works as a cook in a Brahmin household for ₹4,500 a month; three daughters — Anjali (16, Class 11), Sapna (13), Pinky (9) — are in school. Last August, the municipality demolished his thela without notice during a "Smart City beautification" drive. He borrowed ₹40,000 from a local moneylender at 5% per month and rebuilt in three weeks. Three months later, the bulldozer came again — this time stamped "footpath encroachment." The moneylender's debt has compounded to ₹52,000. Account balance is zero. Anjali's Class 11 fees of ₹3,200 are due in December. He writes boastful — twenty-two years, the SP drinks his chai — but the panic underneath is plain. Babu Bhaiya names the structural truth without softening it: a vendor without paper is invisible to the system, and invisible things get bulldozed first. The boast is not a joke, though — twenty-two years of brand equity is the largest single asset in this story, and the entire fight is about getting that brand into the file. The plan runs in two parallel tracks. Track one, paper: register with the Town Vending Committee under the Street Vendors Act 2014 (Sahabganj or Civil Lines office), build a "twenty-two-year occupation" evidence file (old photos, wedding cards mentioning the stall, gas and ration bills, ten signed affidavits from old customers on ₹10 stamp paper), and connect with Janpahal NGO or SEWA Bharat plus the local vendor union to file a collective application from thirty affected vendors directly to the Mayor and DM — single complaints get buried, thirty-vendor files do not. Track two, income now: do not take a second moneylender loan; buy a second-hand cycle-cart for ₹4,000-₹5,000 and run only chai and biscuit for a week — mobility is the whole point until the paper lands. Service the existing moneylender with ₹100-₹200 daily token payments to avoid harassment, and renegotiate later through the NGO under the UP Money-Lenders Act, where unlicensed 5%-per-month lending is not legally enforceable. For Anjali's fees, a written application to the principal citing EWS status, single earner, and demolition impact usually produces an extension or waiver under the Mukhyamantri Kanya Sumangala framework. Raju closes with a real, frugal scheme: three shifts off one cycle — bus stand at 6am, Mohaddipur 9am-4pm, Gorakhpur railway station 9-11pm — and Anjali, on a ₹3,000 second-hand smartphone, runs accounts and a free Paytm QR. The brand is real; the file just needs to catch up.
