सवाल

Khublei. My name is Celestine Lyngdoh, I am 22, from Shillong — Laitumkhrah, near the church. Let me explain my situation slowly because the cultural part matters and people from outside Meghalaya often miss it. We are Khasi, matrilineal. In our system, the youngest daughter is called ka khun khadduh — she inherits the ancestral house, the family land, and she is the one who looks after the parents in their old age. I am ka khun khadduh. I have two elder sisters — both married, one in Guwahati, one in Bengaluru — and the house I am writing this from will, by tradition and by custom, be mine. Along with everything that comes with it.

तो problem यह है — I have an admission letter. NIFT Delhi, fashion design, four-year programme. Offer is real, the seat is mine, fee receipt deadline 21 days से. Total first-year fee around ₹4 lakh. मेरे पास savings हैं from a year of freelance tailoring (Police Bazaar में एक boutique से contract) and Mama (mother's brother — हमारे यहाँ Mama बहुत important figure होता है) ने quietly बोला है कि वो support कर सकते हैं if I ask. Mei (mother) ने letter देखा है — she said nothing for a long time, then said, "Khun, you decide." कोई "हाँ" नहीं, कोई "ना" नहीं. बस "तू decide कर."

Extended family को अभी कुछ नहीं पता. ki kñi (the uncles), the aunts, the cousins — किसी को नहीं. और मुझे पता है क्या होगा. "Ka khun khadduh Delhi में? घर कौन देखेगा? Mei-Pa बूढ़े हो रहे हैं. Khasi लड़कियाँ Delhi से वापस same नहीं आतीं. Land का क्या होगा? Customary law क्या बोलता है?" — यह सब सवाल आएँगे, और तीस साल तक हर शादी में आते रहेंगे.

Sir/madam, I love my family. I love this house. मैं responsibility से नहीं भाग रही. लेकिन मेरे पास एक offer है जो शायद ज़िंदगी में दोबारा नहीं आएगा. 21 दिन हैं. सच बताइए — क्या मैं selfish हूँ अगर हाँ बोल दूँ? और अगर नहीं — तो किसको पहले बताऊँ? कैसे बताऊँ? My hands are shaking while writing this.

— Celestine, Shillong

Summary

Celestine Lyngdoh, 22, from Shillong, is from a Khasi matrilineal family, where the youngest daughter — ka khun khadduh — inherits the ancestral house and is expected to care for her parents in old age. She has an admission letter to NIFT Delhi for a four-year fashion design programme; the fee deadline is 21 days away and she has told no one. Her mother, when shown the letter, was silent for a long time and then said "Khun, you decide" — no yes, no no. Her maternal uncle, who in Khasi tradition holds enormous weight in clan decisions, has quietly offered to help with the fee. The extended family — aunts and uncles — does not yet know, and Celestine is terrified of the thirty years of comments at every gathering that will follow. Rancho refuses the framing of selfishness. People who are actually selfish do not write trembling letters to strangers. The mother's "you decide" is not refusal but a tired Khasi mother's permission — internally yes, externally unable to say it loud, exactly the cadence of women who know the clan will react. The maternal uncle's quiet offer is half the war already won. Rancho also reframes the matrilineal system itself: it exists to give Khasi women power, not trap them; the original purpose of property in the woman's name was to protect her from displacement, not to bolt her into the house. The plan is precise and four-staged within 21 days. First, tonight, alone with Mei at the kitchen table, with the NIFT brochure in hand and concrete details — fee structure, hostel, summer-Christmas visit schedule, plan to bring parents to Delhi next year — extracted from abstract dream into paper. Abstract dreams scare elders; concrete plans don't. Second, the next day, formally ask the maternal uncle whose quiet offer must be converted into an explicit yes — his blessing becomes the shield. Third, a week later, an announcement to the extended family — not a discussion, an announcement — with Mei and Mama already on board, so any aunt-noise is judgement of the periphery, not a decision-blocker. Fourth, a generative angle: study Khasi traditional textiles and eri silk through NIFT's frame, which is both a legitimate cultural framing for the family and a real creative direction. Care for ageing parents is solvable with a paid household help in Shillong, two married elder sisters in Guwahati and Bengaluru rotating during festivals, the maternal uncle nearby, and Celestine returning every three to four months across a four-year course. Raju, who has watched a cousin in Lucknow refuse the same chance and live in the same bedroom twelve years later quietly looking at her own folded letter, closes with the warning: do not make yourself the house and have no room left over for yourself. Pay the fee.
