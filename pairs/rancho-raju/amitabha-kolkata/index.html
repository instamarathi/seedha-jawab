Raju: Amitabha ji, मैं Rancho की हर बात से agree हूँ — पर मैं आपके डर के बारे में अलग कुछ बोलना चाहता हूँ. मैंने अपने पिताजी की बीमारी के सालों में हर रात डर में सोता था — पैसा, दवा, अगली scan की report, क्या बचत बच जाएगी. वो डर मेरा शरीर बन गया था. मेरे दोस्त मुझे बोलते थे "हँस ले यार, अब तो सब ठीक है," और मैं हँसता भी था — पर अंदर डर वहीं था. इसलिए मैं आपको एक बात बहुत respect से बोलूँगा — आपका डर ख़त्म नहीं होगा, चाहे Anirban Pandit बने, चाहे वो worldwide tour करे. आपका डर 1972 से आ रहा है, 2026 के बेटे के Hindustani vocal से वो जुड़ता ही नहीं — आप उसको बेटे के sapne पे project कर रहे हैं. इसका इलाज एक है — डर को अपने पास रखिए, उसकी देखभाल कीजिए जैसे एक बूढ़े दोस्त की करते हैं, पर बेटे को उसका बोझ मत दीजिए. आप 53 के हैं, financial security आपके पास है, ₹20 लाख की salary 7 साल और चलेगी ख़ुदा ने चाहा तो — यह वही समय है जब आप एक generation को डर पास करने के बजाय permission पास करें. और एक last बात — आपकी पत्नी ने एक बहुत precise sentence बोली थी: "मुँह से कुछ और, आँखों से कुछ और." यह sentence Mahasweta का तोहफ़ा है आपको. ज़्यादातर wives यह नहीं बोलतीं. वो आपकी ally हैं इस बात में — उनको साथ बैठाइए जब बेटे से बात करें. वो आपके लिए वो honesty open कर देंगी जो आप अकेले से ला नहीं पाएँगे. Summary

Amitabha Ghosh, 53, is a senior finance manager at an FMCG firm in Kolkata, the son of a 1970s postmaster who paid for every page of his own education by tutoring and clerking at a jute factory. His only son Anirban, 22, in the third year of mechanical engineering at IIEST Shibpur, has revealed that he has been studying Hindustani vocal in secret for four years under a senior disciple of Ustad Rashid Khan, and wants to drop out and pursue music full-time. Amitabha said all the right words — "do what makes you happy, I will support you" — and his face refused to cooperate. His wife caught it that night. The boy has not made eye contact in two days. Amitabha writes asking how to let his son go without robbing him from the inside. Rancho refuses the false binary. The hypocrisy Amitabha is flagellating himself for is not hypocrisy — it is fear pressed flat under love until it warps. The cure is not bigger words; it is a truer conversation. This weekend, with Mahasweta present, Amitabha must do the one thing he never did with his own father: lay his fear bare in front of his son, in clear sentences, without filter. Tell him the 1972 hunger is in his body, not in Anirban's. Tell him the fear is real, but it is the father's fear and the son must not absorb it. Then say, in the same breath, "I am not stopping you. I want you to choose with my fear visible, not dismissed." After truth, structure. Anirban finishes his degree — eighteen more months. The IIEST Mechanical degree is a hedge, not a replacement. Alongside, full Hindustani intensity with Pandit ji. Then a five-year, ₹15-20 lakh "Anirban Music Fund" from Amitabha's savings — not a salary, an art investment with a yearly family review of concerts, students, recordings, AIR auditions, festival appearances. Pre-agreed boundaries, unconditional support. Most importantly, Amitabha must remember the line he himself wrote: he became a CA against his own father's wishes. The Lake Town flat, the corporate income, the entire life that produced his current fear — all of it stands on a rebellion he was permitted to commit. His task now is not to repeat his father's mistake. The job of a generation that swallowed fear is to teach the next one to live alongside fear, not to bow to it. Raju closes with a softer point: Amitabha's fear will not vanish even if Anirban becomes a celebrated vocalist. The fear is from 1972; it has nothing to do with 2026. He must keep it, tend to it like an old friend, but not place its weight on his son. Mahasweta caught the discrepancy because she is his ally; bring her into the conversation, not to mediate, but to hold the honesty steady. The way to let a son go without robbing him is to tell the truth, share the fear, then loosen the grip while staying close.
