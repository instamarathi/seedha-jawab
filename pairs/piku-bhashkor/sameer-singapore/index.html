Summary

Sameer Bhattacharjee, 43, is a Director of Asia-Pacific Risk at a European bank in Singapore. He has lived there eleven years, divorced in 2019, no children. His parents — a retired Tata Motors engineer and a homemaker — are 72 and 69, in good health, in a flat in Aundh, Pune. By every measurable criterion he has done the work of a good son. He transfers ₹2.4 lakh monthly into their joint HDFC account. He calls every Tuesday and Saturday at 8 PM IST, kept on a calendar reminder. He visits twice a year — March for his mother's birthday, October for Durga Puja. He has full medical insurance with a separate cancer rider, an Apollo Pune annual checkup booked, FNP flowers and cake pre-scheduled, his office assistant kept in the loop on the parents' health team. The system is architected. But every call ends the same way — his mother saying "beta, tu yahaan hota toh accha hota," in pleasant tone, no edge, no complaint. He has heard this sentence for eleven years. He sits in his Singapore condo at 10:30 PM with half a whisky and feels he is in a performance review he cannot pass — every KPI green, that one sentence permanently red. He asks, with the discipline of someone used to frameworks, "When is enough enough?" and is afraid the answer is never. Piku and Bhashkor diagnose two separate things at once. Bhashkor recognises the sentence — "tu yahaan hota toh accha hota" — as the Bengali parental signature for "I love you." It is not a complaint, it is not a demand, it is the only way she knows how to say the thing directly. Sameer has been hearing love and parsing it as a deliverable. But Piku adds the harder half: Sameer's mother is not running the scorecard. Sameer is running the scorecard on himself. He has imported a corporate optimisation mindset into a relationship that does not respond to it, and his letter asking for a "framework" is the symptom — what he actually needs is permission to drop the framework. The advice is concrete. First, delete the Tuesday-Saturday calendar reminder; keep only Saturday, and let Tuesday become an unplanned slot for occasional four-minute calls without agenda — "Maa, kuch kha raha tha, aapki yaad aayi" — these out-weigh the scheduled twenty-five minute ones because they are not on a calendar. Second, add a third annual visit, with no occasion attached — not March, not October, just a random ten days in June or February or August, laptop locked in the hotel, no Boat Club meet-ups, no Apollo full-body, just Aundh kitchen and his father's TV remote. The boring trip in which "nothing happened" is the real one. Third, change how he responds to "tu yahaan hota toh accha hota." Stop responding ("haan maa, agli trip jaldi aaunga" is a deliverable) and start receiving — a pause, then "haan maa. mujhe bhi." Nothing else. No commitment, no timestamp, no KPI close. After four or five times of this, his mother will start saying something different — and that something different will be intimacy, not planning. The answer to "when is enough enough" is not "never" — it is "the day you stop scoring." Bhashkor closes with the gentlest line: tell your mother once, in person, looking at her, "Maa, mujhe bhi accha lagta agar main yahaan hota." Then silence. That silence is the eleven-year answer.
