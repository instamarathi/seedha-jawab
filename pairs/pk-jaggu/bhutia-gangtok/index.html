Summary

Tashi Bhutia, 53, retired forest department clerk in Gangtok, lives with his wife on the third floor of a four-storey building in Development Area. He follows the Nyingma school of Tibetan Buddhism. For twenty-five years he has played a quiet 25-minute recorded morning prayer at 6am — Tara mantra, then Vajra Guru, normal volume, well within day-time residential noise limits. Last Saturday his next-door neighbour, who follows the Karma Kagyu school of the same religion, hosted a large puja with a visiting lama from Rumtek. Drums, conch, chanting — full volume — from 6pm to 2am. Eight hours. The shared wall vibrated; his wife's tea cup rattled in its saucer for hours; she got a headache and finally fell asleep around dawn. Bhutia did not complain. He thought of it as a religious occasion. Sunday at 6am he played his normal 25-minute prayer. By 9am the neighbour had complained on the building WhatsApp group: "loudspeaker disturbing morning peace." The secretary backed the complaint and warned Bhutia. Nobody mentioned the eight hours of overnight drumming. His wife, sleep-deprived, read the message and cried. PK frames it with his usual outsider clarity. Both households are Buddhist, both use the same drums, both pray to the same goddesses — yet eight overnight hours is acceptable and twenty-five daytime minutes is a violation. The actual law is the opposite: residential day-time (6am-10pm) permits up to 55 decibels; night-time (10pm-6am) limits to 45. The neighbour broke the rule for four hours; Bhutia did not break the rule at all. The committee responded to volume and event-size, not to fairness. The system PK names: "the louder you are, the more right you become; the quieter you are, the more wrong." The plan is three steps in order. Tonight, Bhutia must sit with his wife, read the message together calmly, and say one sentence: we are not wrong, we will sort this. Her doubt must not be allowed to settle. Tomorrow or the day after, alone, with one cup of tea, Bhutia visits the neighbour — no complaint, no lecture, just a quiet agreement: "tell us when there's a big event, we'll adjust; our daily 25 minutes is twenty-five years old, that stays." Then a calm five-line message on the same WhatsApp group: factual, no accusation, names the eight overnight hours that the building did not respond to, names the twenty-five-year daily practice, requests one uniform standard for everyone. The message protects the two or three other neighbours who were also disturbed by the overnight drums but didn't speak. The secretary will quickly soften because the unfairness is now visible. Jaggu closes with the foundation under all the tactics: a 9am WhatsApp message cannot cancel a twenty-five-year practice that is part of two people's identity. Bhutia's wife needs to hear that tonight, and the rest follows.
