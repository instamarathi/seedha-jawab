Summary

Pooja, 24, a CA articleship student in Vashi, Navi Mumbai, with finals in May 2026, has spent six months engineering twelve "accidental" meetings with Aditya Joshi, 27, a college senior who is now a senior consultant at Cognizant. She maintains a meticulous Excel spreadsheet titled "AJ_Tracker_v3.xlsx" — version 3 because the first two needed reorganisation. Columns include date, day, time, location, engineering method, meeting duration, outfit worn with brand and colour, his reaction on a 1-to-5 scale, conversation length tracked separately from meeting duration, conversation topics, and notes for the next meeting. Two extra tabs hold a Locations Database of 35 places with timings and an Outfit Repeat Tracker. Methods have escalated to a fake ENT appointment at Apollo Vashi, where she paid 850 rupees and Googled "ear pain symptoms" to perform for the doctor, because Aditya is a regular ENT visitor. Aggregate data after twelve meetings: average reaction 2.6 out of 5, total conversation across all twelve meetings 9 minutes 14 seconds, no number ever requested, no Instagram like or DM, only passive story views. She is now planning Meeting #13 around his mother's LinkedIn-derived birthday at the Vashi Crossword. A friend has gently warned her that interest would have produced a number-request by now. She herself asks whether this is romance or "restraining order in progress." Amar and Prem recognize their tribe — they have spent thirty years of their own lives running paper versions of this exact spreadsheet — and refuse to let her continue. The applause is genuine: the methodology is impressive and her CA brain is real. But she has not analysed her own data. Twelve flat data points and a 9-minute total speak the man's language clearly: he is not specifically interested in her, and at 27 in a senior consulting role he is not too shy to act if he were. The plan is in four parts. First, abort Meeting #13 — engineering a date around someone's mother's birthday found via LinkedIn is the line beyond which she will not be able to face herself if it goes wrong. Second, send one clean WhatsApp asking for coffee on Sunday, no engineering, no outfit pre-track, and accept the answer; silence counts as no in this context. Third, actually delete the spreadsheet — recycle bin, cloud backup, Drive history — because keeping it archived is just an excuse for a future relapse, the classic sunk-cost fallacy that Amar and Prem are uniquely qualified to warn against. Fourth, redirect the methodology entirely. CA Final is in six months. Section-wise progress trackers, mock-test scoring databases, case-law judgement trackers, daily revision schedules with traffic-light status — this is the same brain pointed at the right target, and could comfortably take her into AIR top tier. Aditya is not a love story; he is a training data point that taught her methodology alone is not enough, target must be right too.
