Summary

Bindiya Patel, 38, Senior Manager of Regulatory Affairs at a pharmaceutical company in Ahmedabad, financially established with a Bodakdev flat, a CR-V and ₹28 lakh per annum, has been through seventeen matrimonial meetings since age twenty-seven. She maintains a Google Doc — every man's name, education, salary, family, her observations — and a separate "Deal-breakers" tab now four pages long. The list includes the predictable (vegetarian, MBA or engineering, height 5'10"+, salary ₹5 lakh more than hers, ₹2 crore liquid net worth) and the unsettling ("fingers should not be short, I prefer long fingers", neighbourhood preferences mapped down to specific Ahmedabad localities). Last week her mother sat on her sofa, drank tea, and gently said: "Bindi, the problem might be in the list, not the men." Bindiya did not sleep that night. She wants to know which item to remove first and whether there is a method to the demolition. Amar and Prem — themselves connoisseurs of elaborate plans, all of which have failed — recognise the architecture immediately. The list isn't the problem in the way Bindiya is framing it; the list's existence is. Each item was added after a specific meeting injury; the four pages are not preferences, they are scrap from seventeen rounds of self-defence. Armour protects the person inside, but the person inside also stops being seen. Their advice is structural and humane. Do not burn the list — that's drama. Split it. Build a five-item "Core" tab — kindness, honesty, intelligence, age range, vegetarian — and demote everything else into a "Preferences" tab labelled "Bonus, not requirement." Tonight, half an hour. Six sessions with a real therapist — White Swan, Mind Mantra, IAMH — at ₹15-20k will outperform the next seventeen meetings.
