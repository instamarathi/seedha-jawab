22 साल मैंने एक काम किया है — diplomatic रही हूँ. बड़ी जेठानी से सहमत हुई हूँ हमेशा — चाहे menu हो, चाहे बच्चों की पढ़ाई की बात हो, चाहे पूजा का timing. छोटी देवरानी से जब बड़ी जेठानी का जगड़ा हुआ है, मैंने बीच में आकर smooth किया है — दोनों को अलग-अलग बैठा के, "अरे ऐसा नहीं है, वो बस तनाव में थीं." सबके birthdays याद रखे हैं — चौदह लोगों के, बच्चों समेत — हर साल छोटा सा cake, छोटा सा gift. Diwali का खाना मैं 11 साल से बना रही हूँ — पूरे घर के लिए, 30-35 लोग आते हैं रिश्तेदार मिला के. कोई ना नहीं बोला कभी.

"Diwali planning के लिए सबसे helpful कौन?" छह options थे. मेरा नाम भी था. मुझे शून्य vote मिले. शून्य. बाक़ी पाँच में सब के 2-3 votes थे. और फिर उसी ननद ने — जो 2 km दूर रहती है, अपने ससुराल में, और Diwali पे सिर्फ़ खाने आती है, कुछ नहीं करती — comment किया: "Lalitha akka least helpful है, बस smile करती है, सर हिला देती है, अपनी कोई opinion नहीं होती कभी."

तीन हफ़्ते हो गए हैं. मैंने किसी से कुछ नहीं कहा. मेरे पति को भी नहीं. मैं रात में सो नहीं पाती — मन में बस यही चलता है, "22 साल, 11 Diwali, चौदह लोगों के birthdays, सब चाय और smoothing — और शून्य vote." सर, मैं confront करूँ अपनी ननद को? या एक दिन सब कुछ रोक दूँ — एक बड़ा झगड़ा करूँ, सबके सामने, और बता दूँ कि मैंने क्या-क्या किया है? या यह सब बस अंदर रखूँ, अगले Diwali के लिए, फिर वही करूँ? कुछ रास्ता बताइए. मुझे और चुप नहीं रहना है — पर शोर भी नहीं मचाना.

Summary

Lalitha, 47, lives in a 14-person joint family in R.S. Puram, Coimbatore, having married into it twenty-two years ago at the age of twenty-five. For two decades she has executed a careful diplomatic campaign — agreeing with the elder bhabhi, smoothing tempers between sisters-in-law, remembering all fourteen birthdays, never raising her voice during the 2017 chit-fund explosion, and personally cooking the household Diwali for thirty-plus relatives for eleven straight years. Last month the extended family WhatsApp group ran a poll: "Diwali planning ke liye sabse helpful kaun?" Six options. She received zero votes. Her own younger sister-in-law — who lives two kilometres away, contributes nothing, only shows up to eat — commented that Lalitha is "least helpful, just smiles and nods, never has any opinion of her own." She has held this in for three weeks and asks whether to confront the sister-in-law, detonate the diplomacy in one big argument, or swallow it for next Diwali. Amar and Prem — themselves lifelong losers at the project of being liked — diagnose the situation honestly. The family did not vote against her work; they could not see her work, because she vanished into agreement. Twenty-two years of "anything you all decide is fine" trained the household to believe she had nothing to add. Diplomacy without preference reads as absence. Their advice is structural and small. Do not confront the sister-in-law — she is the symptom, not the disease. Do not detonate either — a one-night argument cannot undo a twenty-two-year pattern, and the morning after, she would apologise from sheer habit. Instead, in the next thirty days, voice three small, calm, justification-free preferences: this Diwali's sweets come from KC Das instead of Iyengar's; one vegetable comes off the menu (drop the paneer); on Bhai Dooj this year, the kitchen stays closed because she is tired. One sentence each. No anger. No explanation. Prem closes more softly: people do not remember the helpful, they remember the ones who once asked for something specific. A nine-o'clock filter coffee request, made without being asked, is a smaller act than twenty-two years of cooking — and a thousand times more visible. Lalitha does not need to become more helpful. She needs to become known.
