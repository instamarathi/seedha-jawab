Saurabh, 23, is a Junior Assistant at a PSU bank's Kanpur central branch, drawing ₹32,400 in-hand and living with his parents and younger sister in Govind Nagar. Eight months ago an Australian colleague joined his branch — Hannah, on an 18-month Customer Experience consultant contract via an RBI exchange. Saurabh, on day one, formed a strategy: she is from Australia, Australia means cricket, therefore I will become an Australian cricket expert and the connection will follow. He has been thorough. Steve Waugh, Allan Border, Ricky Ponting, Gilchrist, Bradman's 99.94 — all consumed. He has memorised every Australian Test captain from Dave Gregory in 1877 to Pat Cummins. He maintains an Excel sheet titled "Aus Test Captains 1877-2025" with 47 entries, tenure dates, win-loss records, and personal observations. He took a paid ESPNCricinfo subscription at ₹399, roughly half a day's salary. Every tea break he delivers a new fact at Hannah — Bill Lawry's 1968 defensive style, Mark Taylor stopping at 334, the Greg Chappell underarm controversy. Hannah listens, smiles, says "wow Saurabhji, you really know your stuff," finishes her chai and leaves. Last week he finally summoned the courage to ask her to dinner. She paused, smiled and said: "Oh that's so sweet of the cricket guy! Let me check my schedule." Eight months in, she has not used his name. Amar and Prem, lifelong scheme-makers themselves, recognise the shape of the disaster instantly. The strategy is not just wrong, it is backwards. Saurabh has been performing knowledge AT Hannah, not being curious ABOUT her. In eight months he has not asked her a single real question. He has no idea which Australian city she is from, what she misses about home, what she does outside work, whether she even watches cricket. The cricket research is impressive but it is research about Saurabh, not about Hannah. Their pivot is specific. Tomorrow's tea break: zero cricket facts. One small open-ended question instead — "Hannah, what's the weirdest thing about Kanpur for you, something that wouldn't exist in Australia?" Then listen. The whole chai, listen. One follow-up if something interesting surfaces. Repeat for a week — a new question each day, more listening, more personal. By the third or fourth day Hannah will notice the change and may herself remark that he isn't only the cricket guy. That is the moment. Don't chase the dinner invitation; let it sit. The Excel sheet is not the problem — keep it, expand it, enjoy cricket for its own sake. The problem is using a hobby as a weapon. Performance has never beaten presence, and Amar and Prem submit forty years of failed schemes as proof.
