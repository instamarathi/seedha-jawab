Veerji please, मुझे सीधा बताओ — क्या मैं बेवक़ूफ़ हूँ? या यह सच में चलेगा एक दिन?

Summary

Gurpreet, 29, Brampton — Logistics coordinator, Punjabi family, every Sunday at the big Dixie Gurdwara — has spent six months noticing Jasmeet, a dental hygienist roughly his age. They have spoken once, in the chai serving line, six total words. He had a plan. A grand plan. Cook the perfect sarson da saag, casually deliver to langar, she would notice, conversation would happen, life would change. Batch one: he put corn-roti atta into the saag, it turned black. Batch two: pressure cooker exploded, the kitchen wall is still green. Batch three: tasted to check, accidentally ate the entire pot, slept. Batch four: actually perfect, dropped in the gurdwara parking lot at exactly the moment Jasmeet walked in with her mother. Amar and Prem, who have failed at this exact thing in every available language, recognize a brother. Their advice: stop making saag. Not "make it better" — stop. People with their character drop saag in parking lots; this is a law of nature, not a coincidence. The strategy isn't a grand gesture, it is consistent presence. Sign up for langar seva. Three Sundays in a row, no fuss. Roti-making line or dishwashing — places where Jasmeet, like every Brampton aunty's daughter, will eventually pass through. When she does, no speech, no "scheme" — one "sat sri akaal ji," a small smile, pass her dal. Build recognition over four-five Sundays. Then make one quiet, low-stakes offer — "agle Sunday mere bhua ji hai, kirtan hai 11 baje, agar aap ho aap aana" — information, not invitation. Whatever she does next sets the path. Amar closes with the deeper truth: the obsession with the "perfect" saag was never about Jasmeet. It was Gurpreet trying to earn his own worth. Jasmeet would never have known it took five hours. People don't fall for perfect, they fall for present. Sixty years of failed Amar-Prem schemes are submitted as proof.
