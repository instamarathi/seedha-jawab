Hello. मेरा नाम Shirin है. मैं 7 साल की हूँ. मैं Class 2 में हूँ. Mumbai में रहती हूँ.

मुझे एक problem है. School में बहुत homework मिलता है रोज़. मेरी class teacher Sandhya ma'am हैं. वो ठीक हैं. पर हमारी geography की teacher Mira ma'am हैं — वो ज़ोर से चिल्लाती हैं. कल geography की teacher ने मुझे और दो बच्चों को खड़ा कर के डाँटा क्योंकि हमने pencils share कर ली थी. Aanya की pencil टूट गई थी और मैंने उसको अपनी extra pencil दी थी. ma'am ने बोला हम "cheating" कर रहे थे. हम cheating नहीं कर रहे थे. हम बस pencil दे रहे थे.

मैं school जाती हूँ हर रोज़. Bus में बैठती हूँ subah 7:15 पे. School में 8 से 2 तक. Bus में वापस. घर 3 बजे. जब मैं घर आती हूँ Mummy भी पूछती है पहले homework फिर खाना. कभी कभी मैं चाहती हूँ खाना पहले. पर Mummy गुस्सा हो जाती हैं इसलिए मैं homework करती हूँ. कभी कभी homework में चार books होती हैं. कभी कभी पाँच.

एक बात और. मेरी best friend Aanya है. Aanya भी थक जाती है. हम tiffin break में बात करते हैं — मैं बोलती हूँ "मैं थकी हूँ", Aanya भी बोलती है "मैं भी." पर हमको कोई कुछ नहीं बोलता क्योंकि हम 7 साल के हैं. बड़े लोग बोलते हैं "तुम क्या थक सकते हो? तुम्हारा तो बस school है."

मैं school में बुरी नहीं हूँ. मेरे marks ठीक आते हैं. last test में Maths में 18/20 आए. Hindi में 17. EVS में 19. मैं slow नहीं हूँ. पर मुझे रोज़ रोज़ test जैसा feel क्यों होता है? क्या school को हमेशा hard होना होता है? क्या मुझे यह जो feel हो रहा है ग़लत है?

एक last बात. परसों Mummy मुझे रात को सुला रही थी और मैंने पूछा — "Mummy, क्या मैं ख़ुश बच्चा हूँ?" Mummy ने कुछ नहीं बोला, बस मेरे बाल सहलाती रहीं. फिर मैं सो गई. तो मेरा सवाल है आपसे — मैं ख़ुश बच्ची हूँ या नहीं? और अगर नहीं, तो क्या यह मेरी ग़लती है?

Thank you. आप मुझे जवाब दीजिए please. Mummy मुझे पढ़ के सुनाएँगी.

— Shirin (typed by Nargis), Mumbai

Summary

A letter typed by Nargis, 33, in Bandra East, Mumbai — exactly as her seven-year-old daughter Shirin spoke it, while Shirin slept beside her. Shirin is in Class 2. She gets good marks — 18 in Maths, 17 in Hindi, 19 in EVS. She is not slow, not bad at school. But she is exhausted. The day before, her geography teacher Mira ma'am made her stand up and called it cheating because she had given her broken-pencil friend Aanya her spare pencil. Her own mother makes her do homework before food. At tiffin break she and Aanya tell each other "I am tired" because no adult will accept that a seven-year-old can be tired. Two nights ago Shirin asked her mother whether she was a happy child, and her mother gave no answer, only stroked her hair. Nikumbh refuses to let her be reframed as slow or lazy. The fatigue is real and very specific: she is not tired of school in general, she is tired of every day at school feeling like a test, of every day having to prove she is good. The pencil incident matters even more — she got punished for kindness, and a seven-year-old who learns that kindness is punished will start to suppress kindness as she grows. So Nikumbh writes directly to Shirin, in her own register: she is tez, not slow; her marks are good; the tiredness is real and adults cannot stay tired for long because they have learnt how to invent excuses to stop, but children haven't yet so their tiredness is more honest; sharing the pencil was not cheating, it was kindness, and the teacher's response was the limit of the teacher's understanding, not Shirin's mistake — a sentence she should remember the rest of her life when adults misread her good intent. Her mother's silence the other night was not "no" but "yes" without words — hair-stroking is a language, and Mummy is on her side. Then a brief, direct note to Nargis: trust your daughter — children's tiredness is real, and yours has spotted it precisely; this week, break the homework-first rule on two days; let it be food first, then thirty minutes of drawing, story or singing, then homework — this is oxygen, not indulgence. Email Sandhya ma'am, the class teacher, formally about the pencil incident with a request that small kindnesses not be punished — go via the class-teacher route, not directly to the geography teacher. And the next time Shirin asks if she is a happy child, say something — anything — because silence to a seven-year-old becomes "no." Ishaan closes in seven-year-old solidarity: hold Aanya's hand at tiffin break, she is the other tired child and your army; the adults will fix the rest; and one missed homework night will not bring the sky down — he has tested this many times.
