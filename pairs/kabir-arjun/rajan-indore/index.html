Kabir: Rajan, एक और बात — मेरी तरफ़ से, थोड़ी अलग tone में. आपने letter में एक phrase use किया — "कहीं मैं सोचूँ ही नहीं, और 60 का हो जाऊँ, और तब तक सोचने की उमर ही न रहे." मैं इस phrase को gently challenge करना चाहता हूँ. आप कहीं नहीं गए हैं, Rajan भाई. आपने अभी तक निकलना शुरू ही नहीं किया है — और 48 साल में बहुत देर नहीं हुई है, यह guarantee है. मैं ख़ुद 39 का हूँ, और मैं अभी, इसी हफ़्ते, कुछ नया शुरू कर रहा हूँ जो मुझे 30 की उमर में करना था. Late शुरू करना, ना शुरू करने से हमेशा बेहतर है — यह कोई motivational poster की लाइन नहीं है, यह math है. एक चीज़, इस साल, अपने लिए. वो चीज़ आपकी ज़िंदगी का अगला 20 साल बदल देगी — दुकान को नहीं, माँ को नहीं, बेटी को नहीं — आपको. और आप जब बदलोगे, तब आपके बेटे को एक नया Papa मिलेगा. वही गिफ़्ट है उस सवाल का, जो उसने तीन महीने पहले dinner पे आपको दिया था.

Summary

Rajan, 48, runs Sapna Electricals — an electrical fittings wholesale shop in Sitla Mata Bazar, Indore — that his father started in 1978 and named after his mother. He took it over at 27 when his father had a stroke, and has run it for twenty-one years; his father passed away in 2018, his mother lives in the floor above the shop, his daughter is in Class 11 preparing for medical entrance, his son Aarav is in Class 8. Three months ago, Aarav, doing a Class 7 essay, asked at dinner: "Papa, agar aap kuch bhi kar sakte the, kuch bhi, to kya karte?" Rajan opened his mouth and could not answer. He gave a smart deflection — "main jo kar raha hoon woh hi karta" — and changed the subject. He has not stopped thinking about it since. He has been writing lists at night — bookshop, teaching, music, photography — and crumpling each one because every list ends with "but who will run the shop, my mother lives upstairs, my daughter needs NEET coaching." He writes afraid of both the answer and of never asking. Arjun, who plays the bracingly honest mirror, refuses to send Rajan out of the shop and refuses to let him stay anaesthetised inside it either. The diagnosis: Rajan has been reading the question wrong. Aarav, at thirteen, did not ask about a career change; he asked, in the only words he had, whether his father is anywhere inside the life he runs. Rajan himself has reached this question in his letter. The prescription, in four steps. First, stop making the lists; the lists are paralysing because they are framed as career change, and that isn't the question being asked. Second, in the next thirty days pick one small thing for himself alone — a Friday-evening urdu poetry class, harmonium lessons, two hours weekly volunteering at a library, a ten-day solo trek — chosen by three criteria: nobody asked him to do it, nobody benefits directly from it, and he can skip a week without guilt. That one thing, however small, is his answer. Third, once the thing is real, sit with Aarav and tell him plainly, not as a lesson but as an honest follow-up: I thought about your question for three months, this is the thing I always wanted, I have started. That sentence is more useful to a thirteen-year-old than any career advice. Fourth, the shop is not a prison; it is a context. The mistake of twenty-one years was treating the context as the entire room — one window needs to open, his own. Kabir closes more gently, on age. Forty-eight is not late. Rajan has not gone anywhere — he simply has not started leaving. Late is always better than not at all. One small thing this year, his alone, will change the next twenty.
