Summary

Shekhar Mehta, 66, lives on the 23rd floor in Worli, sea facing. He started in 1979 with a small cloth shop in Bhuleshwar and now runs Mehta Textiles and Mehta Realty — ₹2,400 crore turnover, 800 staff. Six weeks ago at Sunday lunch, his 14-year-old granddaughter Aanya asked, between bites of an apple, what he would do if he lost everything tomorrow. He gave a smart-ass answer. She laughed, finished the apple, and went off on FaceTime. He has not slept properly since. The man who has answered every question of his life cannot answer this one. Arjun, gently but firmly, names what is happening: this isn't about money. Shekhar has merged who he is with what he has built, and Aanya's innocent question forced the gap into the open. The advice is direct. Tell Aanya the truth next Sunday — "I don't know, beta, and finding that out mattered." Then start one small thing that has nothing to do with the business — cooking, gardening, a harmonica, a poem, a daily bicycle ride. No KPI, no output, no one watching. Bhuleshwar 1979 was a feeling of building something without proving anything. That feeling is available again at 66, just outside the ₹2,400-crore frame. Kabir adds: nineteen-year-old energy was really fear in disguise. At sixty-six, what's available instead is curiosity — and curiosity doesn't fear losing things, because it lives in finding new ones. Aanya is the most honest mirror in the house. The window before she leaves for college is short. Use it.
