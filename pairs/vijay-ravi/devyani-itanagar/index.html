Namaste sir, namaste ma'am. मेरा नाम Devyani है, उम्र 17 साल, Itanagar से लिख रही हूँ — Arunachal Pradesh. मैं Nyishi tribe से हूँ. मेरे परिवार में मैं पहली हूँ जिसने Class 12 pass किया है — पापा driver हैं Tata Sumo के, माँ घर का काम और कभी-कभी एक छोटी सी shop चलाती हैं Naharlagun में. मेरे दो छोटे भाई हैं — एक 11वीं में, एक 8वीं में. मेरा subject है Forensic Science. मुझे यह subject Class 9 से पसंद है — उस वक़्त एक NCC camp में एक SP madam आई थीं Itanagar से, उन्होंने एक बार case की कहानी सुनाई थी, और मैंने उसी रात पापा से कहा था — "मैं यह करूँगी." पापा हँसे थे — पर हाँ बोला था.

मेरा PCB का percentage 92% है. मुझे एक State scholarship मिली है — Pradhan Mantri Anusuchit Janjati Vikas Mission के तहत और एक state-level top-up भी, मिलाकर ₹1.2 lakh per year. यह scholarship एक condition के साथ आती है — "candidate must pursue undergraduate course in a recognised institution within the home State (Arunachal Pradesh)." Arunachal में Forensic Science का कोई degree college नहीं है. एक है Rajiv Gandhi University, Doimukh — Forensic Science में सिर्फ़ certificate course है, full BSc नहीं. मेरा subject पढ़ने के लिए मुझे जाना होगा — LNJN National Institute of Criminology and Forensic Science, Delhi, या Amity Noida, या Galgotias. इन colleges की fees ₹2-₹3 lakh per year हैं. मेरे पापा एक साल में सब मिलाकर ₹2.4 lakh कमाते हैं.

एक चीज़ है, sir, जो मुझे रोज़ रात नींद नहीं आने देती. Form में एक column है — "Institution of admission." अगर मैं वहाँ Rajiv Gandhi University, Doimukh लिख दूँ — और सच में Delhi में पढ़ने जाऊँ — scholarship मिल जाएगी. Doimukh में मेरा एक "admission" बनवाना मुश्किल नहीं है — एक चचेरी दीदी वहाँ काम करती है, उनके through एक "name on rolls" हो सकता है, और हर साल का "result" वो माँ को भेज देंगी. यह बात मेरी दीदी ने ख़ुद कही है — "Devyani, छोटी सी बात है." Form एक बार भरना है, और मेरे पास Delhi में पढ़ने के पैसे आ जाएँगे. लेकिन sir... मैं पहली हूँ अपने परिवार में Class 12 pass करने वाली. अगर पकड़ी गई — और कब, कैसे, कौन पकड़ता है यह मुझे नहीं मालूम — तो scholarship गई, degree भी maybe cancel, और मेरा पूरा career का सपना ख़त्म. क्या मैं यह risk लूँ? और अगर न लूँ, तो रास्ता क्या है? मुझे Delhi जाना है, sir. यह subject मेरा है.

— Devyani, Itanagar

Summary

Devyani, 17, is from the Nyishi tribe in Itanagar, Arunachal Pradesh — the first person in her family to pass Class 12. Her father drives a Tata Sumo, her mother runs a tiny shop, and she has two younger brothers in school. She scored 92% in PCB and wants to study Forensic Science, a subject seeded in her by an SP madam at an NCC camp when she was in Class 9. She has a tribal welfare scholarship of ₹1.2 lakh per year — but its rules require her to study within Arunachal Pradesh. The state has no Forensic Science undergraduate degree programme. Delhi has the colleges she needs, but the fees there are more than her father's annual income. A cousin sister has offered her an arrangement: get her name onto the rolls of Rajiv Gandhi University, Doimukh, on paper, while actually attending in Delhi — and keep the scholarship. Vijay and Ravi both feel the pull of that offer, because they understand exactly the corner she is standing in. They do not moralise. They tell her, in plain terms, what the lie costs over a lifetime: Forensic Science means police, courts, government postings — every certificate will be verified, often years later, and the system is forgetful but never amnesiac. Fifteen years of risk for two years of convenience is the worst trade in life. Then they give her two clean roads, to walk in parallel. Vijay's road: file a written representation to the Director of Tribal Welfare and the Ministry of Tribal Affairs invoking the special-case sanction clause; if rejected, take it to the Itanagar Bench of the Gauhati High Court under Article 14 and Article 21 — first-generation tribal Class-12-pass cases have precedent, and pro-bono legal aid is available through NALSA. Ravi's road: actually join BSc Chemistry at Rajiv Gandhi University, Doimukh, top every paper, take Forensic Science certificates online from IGNOU, NFSU and Coursera, and at year two apply for transfer to Delhi or wait for postgraduate scholarships, which carry no home-state restriction. One year's delay is a small price for a clean record that will still be intact when she is forty and her file lands on a younger girl's desk. They tell her how to refuse her cousin sister gracefully, and they tell her the larger truth: she is the elder sibling of an entire neighbourhood now. The foundation she lays cannot be a lie, because every other girl in that mohalla will build on it.
