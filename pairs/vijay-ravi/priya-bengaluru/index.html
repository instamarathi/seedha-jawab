Summary

Priya, 38, a senior reliability engineer in Bengaluru, has spent thirteen years at a US-headquartered medical devices MNC. She is currently on an intra-company transfer assignment in Boston with her husband and nine-year-old son. She signs off on safety reports for an Indian Rural Health Mission tender — eleven thousand portable IV infusion pumps destined for tier-three Primary Health Centres for use on children, pregnant women and dehydration patients. Two weeks ago, in an old version-control folder, she accidentally found the raw data for a critical low-battery flow accuracy test. Twenty-three of fifty units fail the threshold; her manager's signed-off summary records only three "marginal" failures. There is timestamped evidence of the raw data being deleted and replaced, a recording of him telling a junior to leave marginal failures out of the deck, and a complete paper trail on her personal laptop. Flow over-delivery in paediatric IV pumps means fluid overload, pulmonary edema, in worst cases death — and these pumps go to PHCs where no one will audit them. If she reports through internal compliance, her visa sponsorship suspends, she has sixty days to leave the country, and the funding line for her mother's Stage 3 breast cancer treatment in Bengaluru — four and a half lakhs a month — collapses. Her company has a hiring freeze in India. Vijay does not give her an easy answer because there isn't one — both easy answers are lies. He lays out three roads with full bills attached. The truth road: a US whistleblower lawyer this week (Phillips & Cohen, Constantine Cannon and others handle medical-device fraud on contingency or pro bono); SOX Section 806 and FDA Modernization Act Section 17 protect her from retaliation; the lawyer negotiates voluntary resignation with severance, retaliation indemnity and a visa runway, giving six to twelve months of cushion; her mother's treatment is then funded by severance plus husband's salary plus crowdfunding (Ketto, ImpactGuru — a senior engineer's network easily raises fifteen to twenty-five lakhs for a genuine Stage 3 case) plus assistance trusts (Tata Memorial Outreach, HCG, Indian Cancer Society); after twelve months, certain US and EU medical-device firms actively hire whistleblower-track engineers. The anonymous-tip road: CDSCO online complaint portal and FDA MedWatch both accept anonymous reports — saves her job, visa and mother's fund, but anonymous tips move slowly without an accountable reporter, and many pumps will reach PHCs before investigation activates. She will live with that bill in the small hours of the morning, for life. The hybrid road, which Vijay calls smartest: triplicate evidence tonight; anonymous tip filed tomorrow; whistleblower lawyer consultation within forty-eight hours to keep options open; a carefully framed technical "raw data audit request" emailed to a senior officer above her manager — using language inside her job description so retaliation isn't cleanly triggered, but accelerating compliance review enough to hold the eleven thousand pump shipment. This shrinks all three bills at once. Across all three, the foundation is the same and not optional: tonight, encrypt the evidence, triplicate it, and tell her husband everything. The decision should land by Sunday — longer delay only weakens her position as her manager continues to delete what he can. Ravi adds the legal scaffolding: do not put more evidence on the personal laptop from now on, to avoid a "data theft" counter-allegation; Indian Whistleblowers Protection Act 2014 covers central government schemes (this tender qualifies); and the deepest truth — every mother wants only that her daughter be okay, and at peace with herself. Whichever road reconciles Priya with herself is the road. Both Bollywood brothers will be standing with her on whichever one she chooses.
