मोहन जी, एक आख़िरी बात. आपने जो किया वो right था — पर अब उसको right रहने के लिए paperwork चाहिए. आप अकेले एक सरकारी system से नहीं लड़ सकते, और न ही आपको लड़ना चाहिए. System ख़ुद कह रहा है — "इस औरत को बचाओ, हम help करेंगे." बस उस help को activate करना है. इसी हफ़्ते. कल नहीं — आज शाम पहला step. Sunita की safety, और आपकी अपनी नींद, इसी एक call से शुरू होती है.

Summary

Mohan Das, 67, a retired man in Raipur, lives with his wife in a small two-storey house in Shankar Nagar. The upstairs portion is rented out for ₹4,500, which supplements his pension. Nine months ago he rented it to Sunita Kumari, 34, a private school computer teacher who arrived clutching her bag and clearly afraid. His wife, on instinct, told him to give her the room and ask questions later. Two months in, over tea, Sunita explained — five years of beatings from a government clerk husband in Bilaspur, a hospitalisation with a fractured arm, and a quiet escape with no police complaint, no divorce, no relatives told. Five days ago, the husband himself arrived at Mohan Das's gate, a yellow envelope in hand, eyes red, jaw clenched, claiming to be delivering a court summons. Mohan Das felt his heart drop, looked at the man, and the lie left his mouth before he could plan it: "no Sunita lives here." The husband studied him for two minutes, glanced up at the upstairs window, said "let me see," and left. He has not returned. He will. Mohan Das now cannot sleep, asking whether what he did was perjury, obstruction, or right. Vijay says clearly: not a crime. The court summons was being delivered by the husband himself, not a public servant — there is no obstruction of process under section 186 IPC, and perjury requires an oath in court, neither of which applies. Mohan Das saved a night, and a night is not nothing — many violent stories end in the night that nobody refused to open. The lie is consistent with his sixty-seven years of integrity, not against it. But Ravi's intervention is the necessary one. One lie at a gate is improvisation, not protection. The husband will return, perhaps with a real process server, perhaps with police, perhaps in the night. The same protection must now be put on paper. The Domestic Violence Act, 2005 was written precisely for women like Sunita; she qualifies completely. The plan is a single week's worth of steps, in order. Tonight, Mohan Das and his wife sit with Sunita and tell her the truth gently — we are with you, but we cannot keep lying, and there is a lawful path that protects you and us. Then they call 181, the national women's helpline, together, that same evening. The next day, the local Mahila Thana in Raipur, where female officers will file a Domestic Incident Report under the DV Act — the first official paper against the husband. That same week, a women-rights lawyer for ₹2000 or free via Legal Aid, who files a petition for a Protection Order, residence order, and monetary relief before a magistrate. An interim Protection Order can come within seven to fifteen days. Once it arrives, copies sit with Mohan Das, Sunita, and the local thana. The husband cannot then come within a hundred metres without committing a criminal offence; one call to 100 brings police. The conjugal rights summons in the husband's hand is meaningless without Sunita's presence in court, and the moment she appears, her Protection Order petition is already there — his case collapses. Mohan Das did the right thing in the moment. The week ahead is about turning that right thing from a private lie into a public, lawful wall — so that the next knock at the gate is answered with paper, not with fear, and a 67-year-old man can sleep again.
