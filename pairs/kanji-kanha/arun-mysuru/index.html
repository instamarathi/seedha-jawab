Summary

Arun, 70, retired physics professor in Mysuru, has been a public rationalist for fifty years — newspaper columns, science forums, exposing godmen, ridiculing rituals from stage. His wife Shanta, religious by temperament, was his counterpart in a fifty-year armistice of mutual teasing and deep love. She died of cancer eleven weeks after diagnosis, in his hand at AIIMS Bengaluru. At her funeral, Arun performed every Hindu ritual — pinda, the crow on the roof, all thirteen days, ashes in the river — with complete sincerity. Not going through the motions; really doing them. He writes asking what that means about him: was his rationalism intellectually weak, did grief expose a scared man underneath, what does he tell his readers, what does he tell himself? Kanji, the rationalist shopkeeper who happens to sell idols, refuses to let him punish himself. Funeral rituals, Kanji explains, are not about belief. They are choreography for grief. Words fail at that scale; hands and bodies need something to do; thirteen days of structured action let the mind break safely. Arun did not betray fifty years of reason — he discovered its honest limit. Reason is the right tool for science. It is not the right tool for losing the person you have loved for fifty years; nothing is, but ritual is the closest thing humans have built. Performing it in his wife's language was not capitulation, it was respect for a fifty-year marriage. Kanha, sitting beside Kanji as a quiet guest, adds the line that closes the case: the rituals did not catch Arun. His wife caught him. What he was doing through pinda and ash and water was not theology — it was the last shape of a touch that had reached him for fifty years. There is no shame to apologise for, no column to write, no readership to face. Only one small thing: a cup of tea at six in the morning, looking towards her chair, and the words "I sent you well." That is enough, and it is whole.
