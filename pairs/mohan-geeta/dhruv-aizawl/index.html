सवाल

नमस्कार. मेरा नाम Dhruv Lalchhanhima है, उम्र 25, Aizawl के पास एक छोटे गाँव — Sialsuk — से लिख रहा हूँ. मेरे parents subsistence farmers हैं, मैं अपनी family का पहला graduate हूँ — Mizoram University से B.Tech (Civil), 2023 में. एक बात पहले — मेरी community में मेरे जैसे लोगों के लिए "कुछ बड़ा करना" का मतलब अक्सर एक चीज़ होता है: leave. Aizawl से Guwahati, Guwahati से Bangalore या Delhi. मैंने वो रास्ता नहीं लिया. Graduation के बाद मैं वापस Sialsuk आया — एक specific reason से.

2023 में, गाँव की पुरानी water supply collapse हो गयी थी — एक PWD pipeline थी, 1990s की, जो leak कर रही थी और last रोज़ 6 घंटे ही पानी देती थी. Children को 4 km चढ़ कर एक झरने से पानी लाना पड़ता था. मैंने अपनी degree का use करके — और हमारे District Council से एक छोटा grant ले के, ₹2.8 lakh — एक gravity-fed system डिज़ाइन किया. Hilly terrain में जो ऊपर का spring था (570 metres elevation, गाँव से 1.4 km), उसे एक filtration tank से connect किया, फिर PVC pipes से 8 distribution points तक, हर household-cluster को daily 12 hours flow मिलता है, treated, free. यह अब लगभग दो साल से चल रहा है. कोई contractor नहीं — सब community labour, मैंने technical design किया, training दी, और अभी maintenance schedule मैं ही manage करता हूँ — turbidity check हर हफ़्ते, monsoon में silt clearance, dry season में spring flow monitoring. लगभग 1,200 लोग depend करते हैं इस system पर.

पिछले महीने Bangalore की एक tech firm से offer आया — Junior Project Engineer, Civil-Tech, ₹14 LPA. मेरे parents की पूरी ज़िंदगी की कमाई से ज़्यादा यह एक साल का salary है. मेरी छोटी बहन Class 11 में है, उसकी fees, माँ की diabetes की दवा, घर का छप्पर जो पिछले monsoon में टूटा था — यह सब इस salary से solve हो जाता है. और मेरा अपना career — मैं 25 का हूँ, यह अभी का सही moment है आगे बढ़ने का.

लेकिन यह water system. अगर मैं चला गया — कौन maintain करेगा? मैंने अभी तक किसी को train नहीं किया properly. Spring flow gauge पढ़ना, चlorination dosing calculate करना, monsoon में emergency rerouting — यह सब मेरे सर में है, written down नहीं. अगर मैं Bangalore में हूँ और कोई pipe burst हो जाए, मैं remote से कितना help कर सकता हूँ?

मैं permission नहीं माँग रहा. मैं यह नहीं पूछ रहा कि कौन सा right choice है. मैं बस यह जानना चाहता हूँ — कैसे जीना है इस choice के बाद, दोनों ही cases में. अगर रहूँ, तो उस ₹14 LPA को कैसे miss करूँ — माँ की दवा, बहन की पढ़ाई. अगर जाऊँ, तो उस system को कैसे छोड़ूँ — और अगर वो fail हो जाए, तो उस failure का हिस्सा कैसे carry करूँ. आप दोनों practical बात बताइएगा, please.

— Dhruv, Aizawl

Summary

Dhruv Lalchhanhima, 25, is the first graduate from a poor farming family in Sialsuk village near Aizawl, Mizoram. After completing a Civil Engineering degree from Mizoram University in 2023, he returned home — not as default but as a specific decision. The village's old PWD water pipeline had collapsed; he secured a ₹2.8 lakh grant from the District Council, designed a gravity-fed system using a spring 1.4 km away at 570m elevation, and over community labour built it out. The system now delivers 12 hours of daily treated water to roughly 1,200 villagers across eight distribution points. He has run maintenance personally for almost two years. Last month a Bangalore tech firm offered him ₹14 LPA — more than his parents' lifetime earnings combined, money that would solve his mother's diabetes medication, his sister's Class 11 fees, and the broken roof from last monsoon. But the maintenance schedule lives in his head — not written down. He is not asking which is the right choice; he is asking how to live with either. Mohan and Geeta refuse the framing. The Bangalore-vs-village question is the wrong question right now. The real engineering failure is that Dhruv has built a system with a single point of failure: himself. Any cyclone, any accident, any emergency that pulls him away for 24 hours puts 1,200 people at risk — that is unacceptable today, irrespective of any job offer. The advice is concrete and time-bound. First, identify two people in the village this week — one mid-30s for stability, one early-20s for succession — both literate, both staying, both interested in technical work. Second, document everything in six weeks: a binder in Mizo and Hindi (not English) with photos, covering spring flow measurement, filtration cleaning, pipe network map and valve protocols, chlorination dosing, supplier and grant officer contacts, monsoon emergency protocols. Third, four months of hands-on training with monthly "Dhruv disappears" drills where the two trainees handle a real week without him. Fourth, only after that, the choice. If the system can survive two weeks without him, both options become free choices rather than fragile ones — Bangalore becomes a career move from a stable system, staying becomes a positive bet on replicating the design in two or three other villages via NGO grants (Tata Trust, Azim Premji Foundation). On Bangalore: ask the company for a four-to-six month joining buffer; if they refuse, the offer was not for him anyway. On the income gap if he stays: NESAC, NEEPCO, and hill area development board positions in Aizawl pay ₹6–8 LPA — interim, weekend-commute possible, real postings, not fantasy. Geeta closes with the harder truth from thirty years of teaching: a former student of hers nearly skipped her IAS scholarship because "if I leave, who is here?" — and twenty-five years later it was her leaving that brought the village its primary health centre. The village does not need indispensability; it needs the best version of Dhruv, and the best version cannot stay rooted at 25.
