Summary

Manvendra Singh Bajwa, 44, is an Indian Forest Service officer of the 2003 batch in the Punjab cadre, currently Conservator of Forests, living in Sector 9, Chandigarh, with his wife Manjeet (a Hindi professor at Punjab University) and two school-age children. His oldest friend, Karandeep, is an IPS officer of the same 2003 batch — they met at LBSNAA Mussoorie twenty-two years ago. For two decades they have met every Tuesday at 6:30pm in a small café near Sector 17's Indian Coffee House: two chai, one plate pakora. Last month Karandeep was elevated to Additional Director General of Police; in the same window Manvendra's name was left off the final list for Principal Chief Conservator of Forests, the equivalent IFS rank. The bureaucratic reasons are layered — internal politics, representation, possibly an old public-interest litigation he had ruled against the government on. Karandeep is not the cause. But the timing has placed an elephant in the middle of their café table. Three Tuesdays have passed. Neither has said a word about it. Five-minute silences have appeared between safe topics — children's exams, Punjab weather, books, his wife's travel. Manvendra is clear it is not jealousy and not shame; he names a third thing — pride that will not let him speak his own pain — and writes worried less about his career than about whether the friendship survives. Jai diagnoses it precisely: pride is the other name of pain, especially among male friends of long standing, especially among civil servants who file briefings about everything except themselves. The rule of who breaks first is simple — whoever misses it more, and that is Manvendra, because he is the one writing this letter. Next Tuesday, before Karandeep speaks, deliver one structured sentence: "Karandeep, three weeks late, congratulations on ADGP. And — my name didn't make the PCCF list. This elephant has been between us for three weeks. Today I'm removing it. Your promotion is not my problem. My miss is not yours. They happened in the same window, that's coincidence, not a friendship issue." No emotional speech, no "you deserved it," no self-pity — civil-servant-grade clarity, the way a briefing is delivered. Either Karandeep relaxes immediately and admits he too didn't know how to bring it up, or he stays awkward — in both cases, the Tuesday chai survives, and the long silences end. One condition: never raise this again. One day of honesty, then twenty-two more years of friendship — repeated mention turns honesty into self-pity that slowly poisons the relationship. And one practical instruction: tonight, tell Manjeet, in private, in one sentence — she is a Hindi professor, words are her work, she will understand better than expected. A man with one hand on his shoulder stands longer. Veeru closes with the truth no one in the civil service will say out loud: at forty-four, a twenty-two-year friend is the only person you can be entirely yourself with, and that becomes more, not less, valuable in the next twenty years. A list cannot end this. Only silence can. Open the mouth on Tuesday — Karandeep will open his within half a second, because he has been waiting for exactly this.
