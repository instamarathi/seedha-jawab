Summary

Geeta Devi, 38, a government middle-school Hindi and Social Science teacher in Patna's Kankarbagh, writes about her closest friend from Patna Women's College — Sunita, with whom she shared notes through their BEd, walked daily to the Bailey Road bus stand, stayed three nights at her wedding. Three years ago at Geeta's own reception, exhausted and stretched thin, the two women fell into a twenty-minute argument that began with a small comment about Geeta's sari or in-laws and escalated into things neither had ever said in a decade — about Geeta's late marriage, about Sunita's husband. Sunita walked out mid-reception. Three years of strict silence have followed. Both have new colleagues, new circles. Neither has blocked the other. Geeta still dreams, sometimes, of the two of them walking to the bus stand together, schoolbags on their shoulders, talking about an exam. She wakes and stares at the ceiling. Jai diagnoses it cleanly: this is not a dead friendship, it is a paused one — both women are waiting for an apology that confirms their own version of the fight, and as long as both wait, neither moves. Don't apologise — for what you said was your own partial truth, and a fake apology will turn into self-resentment within two weeks. Instead, today, send a four-line WhatsApp message: there was a misunderstanding at the reception, you've been thinking about it, would she be open to chai sometime, no pressure. Don't litigate the fight. The point is to walk past the gate, not knock it down. When they meet, do not raise that evening — if Sunita does, deflect with "we were both exhausted that night, let it go." Three years on, the fight matters less than the fact that both women still walk down Bailey Road in their dreams. Veeru closes with the urgency: the message must go today, not "soon" — every "soon" that postpones becomes a day that never arrives, and the regret of hearing her name from a stranger is the regret to be avoided. The dream is the key on the chair; today, pick it up.
