BP कब last check किया था? यह सब hypothetical नहीं हैं — chronic low-grade stress के यह असली physical signs हैं, और इनको notice करना कोई "soft" काम नहीं, यह बिल्कुल capable वाला काम है, सिर्फ़ अपने ऊपर apply किया हुआ. एक बार GP से एक basic check-up — CBC, thyroid, vitamin D, BP — करवा लीजिए अगले महीने में अगर तीन साल से नहीं हुआ है. एक और suggestion — option के तौर पर, instruction के तौर पर नहीं. AIIMS Bhubaneswar में psychiatry OPD है, एक session structured बात के लिए — कोई diagnosis नहीं, कोई label नहीं, सिर्फ़ एक trained इंसान के सामने एक घंटा. वो आपको बता पाएँगे कि वो 90 मिनट क्या थे, और आगे चाहे तो technique भी देंगे — pacing, breathwork, कुछ simple. यह आपकी "manager" वाली ability का negation नहीं है, यह उसका extension है — एक well-organised account अपने भी रख रही हैं, papa की नहीं, माँ की नहीं — अपनी. सोचिएगा. कोई rush नहीं. बाबू-मोशाय Anand कह रहे हैं, मैं भी कह रहा हूँ — आप अकेली नहीं हैं, और वो 90 मिनट कुछ नहीं नहीं थे, वो बहुत कुछ थे.

Summary

Usha, 41, is a senior accountant in Bhubaneswar, married with two school-age children, and the eldest daughter of a retired PWD civil engineer. Three years ago her father was diagnosed with Stage IV pancreatic cancer at AIIMS Bhubaneswar; six months later he was gone. Across the diagnosis, the chemotherapy attempts, three siblings flying in from Bangalore, Delhi and Pune, the cremation at Swargadwara, the asthi-visarjan at Puri, the 13-day shraadh, the death certificate, the bank closures, the LIC policies, the PWD pension transfer to her mother (which took 14 trips to the Bhubaneswar AG office), Usha managed everything. She did not cry once. Last Saturday she went to her mother's house in Old Town to retrieve a school certificate, opened a drawer in her old bedroom, and found her father's brown wire-frame reading glasses — the ones he wore at 7am to read the Sambad. She picked them up, sat on the floor, and stayed there for ninety minutes. She did not cry. She did not think anything specific. She got up, put the glasses back, made normal conversation with her mother, went home, ate dinner, slept. She does not believe she is "in denial" — she is genuinely functional — but she does not know what those ninety minutes were, and asks whether she should be doing something. Anand and Bhaskar tell her she did not skip grief; she postponed it, and three years later it came knocking with a pair of glasses in its hand. Tears are one form of grief; sitting on the floor with a dead father's reading glasses is another. Both are real. The ninety minutes were not nothing. They were the first time in three years she let herself stop being the manager. Four things, all permission rather than checklist. One, do not try to manufacture tears — grief is not an audit; recognise what already happened, do not perform what hasn't. Two, keep the glasses somewhere findable, not on display and not buried — a shelf, a small box — and go back sometimes, sit, do not translate the silence into a paragraph. Three, tell one person this week — husband, younger sister, an old friend who knew the father — one sentence: "I sat on the floor with Papa's glasses for ninety minutes on Saturday." No explanation, no apology. Naming it once outside her own head will let it become a memory rather than a mystery. Four, give it six months; if a frozen place remains, then think about a counsellor — not before, not as a failure. Bhaskar adds the medical: three and a half years of sustained high-functioning stewardship leaves chronic low-grade physical traces — sleep, BP, neck and jaw tension, digestion. A basic GP workup is overdue. As an option (not an instruction), one structured session at the AIIMS Bhubaneswar psychiatry OPD — not as diagnosis, just as a hand on the shoulder — could give her language for what the ninety minutes were. The capable daughter and the daughter on the floor are the same woman. Quiet grief is also grief.
