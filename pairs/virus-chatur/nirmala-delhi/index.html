एक आख़िरी बात — choice आपके सामने है, और यह बहुत साफ़ है. आप वो माँ बन सकती हैं जिसके पास Aditya अपने बच्चों को proudly लाएगा — "देखो, यह मेरी माँ हैं, इन्होंने मुझे बड़ा किया." या आप वो माँ बन सकती हैं जिसको Aditya manage करता है — एक monthly duty visit, बच्चों को कुछ हफ़्ते पहले prep करते हुए कि "grandma कुछ बातें करती हैं, ignore करना." पहली वाली माँ बनने के लिए आज आपको pricing बंद करनी है, Suchitra को एक 5-minute acknowledgement देना है, और Aditya को partner बनाना है. दूसरी वाली बनने के लिए कोई काम नहीं चाहिए — वो default direction है जहाँ आप already जा रही हैं. अगले 6 महीने decide करेंगे कि कौन सी route पकड़ी गयी है. आप ख़ुद decide कीजिए — मैं decide नहीं कर सकता.

Chatur: Mrs. Singhania, मैं Chatur हूँ — एक ICE alumnus, अब US में हूँ, salary बहुत अच्छी है, condo है, सब है. मेरी तीसरी fiancée Sweetie ने पिछले साल break-up के बाद मुझे बोला था — "तुम्हारे साथ मैं कभी enough feel नहीं करती." मैंने उसको lunch पर एक बार Manhattan condo की maintenance fees बताई थी — exact dollar number. एक बार ही, मैंने सोचा "casual mention." Six महीने बाद वो बात उसके दिमाग़ में बैठी थी — "Chatur के साथ मैं छोटी हूँ, हमेशा रहूँगी." यह pattern आप और मैं दोनों ने share किया है — pricing करना, और सोचना कि यह "information" है जब वो actually एक wall है. मेरा सर ने जो कहा वो सही है, और एक बात मैं जोड़ूँगा — आप 63 की हैं, मैं 38 का हूँ. हम दोनों के पास change करने का time है — आपके पास Aditya के बच्चे आने से पहले, मेरे पास अगली relationship से पहले. यह change comfort वाली नहीं है, मानता हूँ. पर यह "मेरे साथ कोई enough feel नहीं करता" वाली life से बेहतर है, क़सम से बेहतर है. आपकी बहू को आपकी Banarasi की क़ीमत नहीं चाहिए — उसे आपकी एक छोटी सी कहानी चाहिए कि कैसे आप ने 1985 में पहली बार Banaras गयीं और कैसे एक weaver ने आपको silk के 12 grades समझाये थे, कैसे साढ़े तीन घंटे बैठ कर देखा था एक particular pattern बनते हुए. यह information है, यह love है, यह heritage है — और यह free है, इसमें कोई rupee नहीं है. आप इसी version की Mrs. Singhania बन सकती हैं — आज से, इस evening से. Best of luck.

Summary

Nirmala Singhania, 63, writes from a Lutyens bungalow on what she still calls Aurangzeb Road. She is old Delhi money — Singhania Sugar Mills heritage on her father-in-law's side, a Banarasi worth ₹3.4 lakh on her shoulders, a Kashmiri tanpura from 1962 in her drawing room, a son who went IIT-Stanford-McKinsey-now-startup and married three years ago. The daughter-in-law, Suchitra, is from a middle-class Pune family — Air Force father, school principal mother, M.Sc. in biotech, now a senior pharma executive earning roughly what her husband earns. At family dinners that Nirmala hosts two or three times a month, Nirmala "shares information": the price of her Banarasi, the provenance of the tanpura, the carat of the family rings. Over the past eight or nine months, Suchitra's face has begun changing during these mentions; she has started going quiet and shifting topics. Last month Nirmala announced to her sister-in-law, in front of Suchitra, that "in our family rings are 21 carat, not 18, the daughter-in-law should know this." That night Aditya came to her room and said, "Mom, please don't do that again. Suchitra felt humiliated." Nirmala, who frames herself as reasonable and her DIL as the problem, writes asking why her son has started taking his wife's side about everything. Virus does not flatter her. The answer is brutally direct: this is not a daughter-in-law problem; this is a "you mentioned the price of your saree at three dinners and your son finally noticed" problem. Aditya is not switching sides; he is growing up, late at thirty-six. Intent and impact are different — the intent of "information sharing" does not negate the impact of a wealth gap reminder delivered to a middle-class-origin DIL at every meal. The 21-carat ring announcement was not innocent observation; it was a public, formal, hostile action whose target was identifiable. The framing of self-as-reasonable while being the actor is the textbook definition of wounded entitlement. The advice is in three concrete moves. First, stop pricing things — every Banarasi has a story about the weaver and the silk grades, but the rupee number never leaves the mouth again, starting today; this rewires forty years of identity and will be hard. Second, have a five-minute private conversation with Suchitra — no Aditya in the room — and acknowledge, without "but," that the dinner-table comments have been a weight; this is not apology so much as recognition, and it can become a founding moment for the saas-bahu relationship. Third, talk to Aditya not about Suchitra but about herself — "I have noticed a pattern in myself, I want to change it, tell me when I slip" — which frees him from picking sides and turns him into a partner in the change. The closing frame is the choice: Nirmala can become the mother whose grandchildren are proudly brought to her, or the mother whose son manages her as a monthly duty visit and prepares the kids in advance to ignore certain comments. The default direction — without doing anything — is the second one. Six months will decide which route Aditya's family settles into. Chatur closes by recognising the same pattern in himself: a single mention of his Manhattan condo's maintenance fees lodged in his ex-fiancée's mind for six months as "I am not enough." Pricing is a wall, not information. Suchitra does not want the rupee value of the Banarasi; she wants the story of the weaver and the afternoon Nirmala sat watching one specific pattern emerge. That version of Nirmala costs nothing and is the one her son's family can live alongside.
