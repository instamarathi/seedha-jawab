Virus: Surekha, सबसे पहले एक बात साफ़ — आप अच्छी होनी बंद नहीं हुई हैं. आप अच्छी हैं — यह आपकी letter में आपने ख़ुद written है, और मुझे आप पे विश्वास है. आपने सिर्फ़ दो अलग-अलग jobs को आपस में मिला दिया है, और इस गड़बड़ी से रात की नींद ख़राब हो रही है. एक job है — best technician की job. वो tool चलाना, dashboard बनाना, macro लिखना, Python notebook में filter लगाना. यह आपकी 12 analysts की job है, उनको इसके लिए hire किया गया है, उनकी salary इसके लिए है. दूसरी job है — best judge की job. कौन सा client portfolio safe है, कौन सा रिश्ता बनाना है, कौन सी risk लेनी है, किस number के पीछे कौन सी कहानी छुपी है — यह आपकी job है, और यही 18 साल की experience का असली काम है. आप पिछले महीने से technician वाली job में फँस गई हैं — और judge वाली job को नज़रअंदाज़ कर रही हैं. यह correction है, defect नहीं.

Summary

Surekha, 43, is the first person in her family to reach Vice President — at a private financial services firm in Chennai. Her father retired as a school clerk; her mother is a homemaker. She manages twelve analysts, mostly between 27 and 34, fluent in tools she had to learn after her promotion: Tableau, advanced Excel macros, a couple of Python notebooks. She is still excellent at the job she was actually hired for — relationship-led client portfolios, judgement, long-arc decisions. Last month, in a team meeting, a 28-year-old analyst asked her to pull up a specific Tableau view and she froze. She fudged it with "let's discuss this offline." Since then she has stopped opening her laptop in front of her team, tilts her screen in the cabin, Alt-Tabs when anyone passes. She fact-checks every paragraph of every email three times before sending — sometimes till 1 a.m. Her thirteen-year-old daughter has noticed. She is asking when exactly she stopped being good and started only needing to seem good. Chatur diagnoses imposter syndrome with corporate confidence and is wrong. Virus disagrees firmly: this is not imposter syndrome; this is the right structure of seniority misread as failure. The senior's job is judgement, not the junior's tools — that is precisely what the team is for. The mistake is not the gap; the mistake is hiding the gap. The fix is structured and small. Once a week, in the team meeting, ask one analyst to walk her through their tool, framed as "I want to understand the constraints you are working with." Stop fact-checking emails three times — the cost in sleep and her daughter's bedtime is higher than the cost of any typo. Hold a quiet conversation alone about the difference between being good and being current — twelve juniors will always be more current than one senior; that is the org chart, not a personal failure. And, most importantly: hidden mistakes compound; one openly-acknowledged gap closes faster than ten covered ones. Chatur closes by accidentally landing the truest line of the column: if you laugh first, the rest of the room goes silent.
