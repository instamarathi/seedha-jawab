सवाल

Vanakkam, sir. My name is Saroja, I am 72 years old, from Tirunelveli — small town in Tamil Nadu, near the Thamirabarani river. My English is okay-okay only, my Hindi I learnt from old Doordarshan serials, so please adjust. I will tell you my problem in plain words.

For thirty years, sir, I ran the biggest saree shop in our district — Saroja Saree Centre, on East Car Street, near the Nellaiappar temple. My husband started it, he passed in 1996, after that I ran it alone. Three sons, all educated, all settled. The youngest one, Karthik — engineer, but he loved the shop — he came back from Chennai in 2018 and started helping. In 2020 I retired, gave him the shop. Six years now, sir. He has done — ennada solradhu — wonderful things. Made a website. Started Instagram, sir, can you believe it. Sells to ladies in Singapore, Dubai, Malaysia. Revenue double, employees double — fourteen now, was seven in my time. Last Diwali he gave me a paper — chart with colours — "Amma, see, your shop is a success." I cried. I cried whole evening. He thought happy tears. They were not fully happy, sir. I will tell you the truth, even if it is ugly. For thirty years I thought the shop runs because of me. My memory of every customer, my eye for what saree suits whom, my voice telling weavers in Kanchipuram what to dye. I thought without me — collapse. Six years gone. No collapse. Better than before. Sir, I am proud. Don't think I am not. But what is this small ache in my chest that won't go? Po — let it go, I tell myself. It does not go. I sit on the thinnai every evening, watch the street, and a thought comes — "all those years, I was not as necessary as I believed." Is this the truth? Or is this old-age madness? Please give me one straight answer.

— Saroja, Tirunelveli

Virus: Saroja जी, vanakkam. आपने जो सवाल पूछा है — "क्या मैं उतनी ज़रूरी नहीं थी जितना सोचा था?" — यह सवाल सच्चा है, और इसलिए इसका जवाब भी सच्चा होना चाहिए. हाँ, आप उतनी indispensable नहीं थीं जितना आपने सोचा था. कोई नहीं होता. हर दुकान, हर college, हर office एक दिन उस इंसान के बिना चलती है जिसने उसको बनाया था — यह जीवन का नियम है, यह आपकी कमी नहीं है. लेकिन — और यह बहुत बड़ा "लेकिन" है — आप 'indispensable' नहीं थीं, पर आप 'meaningful' थीं. ये दो शब्द एक नहीं हैं. आपने तीस साल के काम में Karthik को एक ऐसा बेटा बनाया जो Instagram पे साड़ी बेच सकता है — पर साड़ी की समझ आपकी है, साड़ी का नाम आपका है, साड़ी का reputation आपका है. वो website "Saroja Saree Centre" लिखती है — यह नाम वहाँ आपके बिना नहीं होता.

अब practical बात — क्योंकि philosophy से अकेले thinnai का ख़ालीपन नहीं भरता. एक — हर हफ़्ते एक काम करिए: एक पुराना customer बुलाइए, चाय पे, घर पे. वो आएंगे — Tirunelveli में लोग आते हैं, यह छोटा शहर है, रिश्ते जीवित हैं. वो साड़ी की दो-तीन कहानियाँ बताएंगे आपकी, जो शायद आप भी भूल चुकी हैं — "अम्मा, उस दिन याद है, आपने मेरी शादी के लिए वो green Kanchi निकाली थी पीछे से?" यह आपकी छाप है, यह दूसरों के पास है — आप अकेले उसको रखने नहीं बैठीं. उसको collect करना, सुनना, ख़ुश होना — यह retirement का असली काम है.

दो — पोते-पोतियों के साथ बैठिए, हर दूसरे रविवार. हर साड़ी की एक कहानी है — कौन सा weaver, कौन सा गाँव, कौन सी dye, कौन सा pattern, और किस ज़माने में कौन सा design popular था. यह history है, और यह history आज की generation को कोई नहीं सिखाता. यह transmission है — आपका असली contribution, जो website पे नहीं चढ़ सकता. Karthik साड़ी बेच रहा है; पर साड़ी की कहानी आप ही बता सकती हैं. यह काम आप अकेले कर सकती हैं — दुनिया में सिर्फ़ आप. यह indispensable है — पर revenue में नहीं, memory में.

और तीसरी बात, जो सबसे ज़रूरी है — Karthik को बोलिए, सिर्फ़ एक बार: "बेटा, मुझे थोड़ा अजीब लगता है shop को इतना बड़ा देख के. खुश हूँ, पर अजीब भी." यह बात बच्चा सुनेगा, और बच्चा कुछ बेहतर करेगा — हर महीने एक बार आपको shop में लेके आएगा एक घंटे के लिए, सिर्फ़ चाय पीने. कोई काम नहीं — सिर्फ़ बैठना. East Car Street की धूल, weavers का phone, customers की हँसी — यह आपके अंदर है, आप जब वहाँ बैठेंगी, आधा ख़ालीपन उसी दिन भर जाएगा.

Summary

Saroja, 72, in Tirunelveli, ran the district's biggest saree shop on East Car Street near the Nellaiappar temple for thirty years. Her husband started it in the 1970s; he died in 1996; she ran it alone after that. In 2020 she handed it to her youngest son Karthik. Six years on, the shop has a website, an Instagram presence, customers in Singapore and Dubai, fourteen employees instead of seven, and double the revenue. She is proud. She is also, quietly, devastated — she had built her sense of self on the belief that the shop ran because of her. It does not. It runs better. Sitting on her thinnai every evening, she keeps thinking: "I was not as necessary as I believed." Virus, recently retired from Imperial College, and Chatur, a US-based VP, both flinch at the question because it is theirs too. Virus draws the distinction that finally lands: indispensable and meaningful are not the same. Indispensable means the system breaks without you — that is design failure, not achievement. Meaningful means you left a mark the system carries forward. Saroja's saree-eye, her weaver-relationships in Kanchipuram, her thirty-year customer trust — all of it is in Karthik's website without her name on it. The advice is concrete. Invite an old customer for tea every week — the stories they bring back are the proof. Sit with grandchildren on Sundays and tell them the history of every saree, every weaver, every dye — this transmission is the work no website can do, and only she can do it. And tell Karthik, once, plainly, that the shop's bigness feels strange. He will start bringing her in for a chai every month. The thinnai will fill, slowly, with what was always hers — not the shop, but its memory.
