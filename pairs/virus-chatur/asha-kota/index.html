Summary

Asha, 17, is in her second year of JEE coaching in Kota. Her father, a primary school teacher, sends ₹22,000 of his ₹38,000 salary every month to keep her there. Her roommate Priyanka, from Madhubani, has placed ahead of her in every mock test for six months — the gap has widened from 200 ranks to 800. Asha now copies Priyanka in everything: she reads her notes at night while she sleeps, copies her handwriting, wakes at 4:30 because Priyanka does, has stopped chai because Priyanka doesn't drink it, has stopped roti because Priyanka eats only dal-chawal. She has stolen Priyanka's pen. She writes that she hates the Asha she used to be. Virus, the man who once threw a student's poetry in a bin, and Chatur, who built his entire adult life on copying a roommate named Rancho, both recognise themselves in this letter and admit it for the first time. The advice is sharp and personal. Stop reading Priyanka's notes. Make your own, even if they are ugly. Return the pen tomorrow with a single sentence apology — a guilty mind does not pass any exam. Drink your chai again. Eat your roti again. Every night, ask one question: did I move ahead in Asha's syllabus today, or in Priyanka's? If the second, fix it tomorrow. The father's money is not wasted yet — it will only be wasted if she erases herself before the exam. Comparison is a disease that starts at seventeen and runs for life. Stop it tonight.
