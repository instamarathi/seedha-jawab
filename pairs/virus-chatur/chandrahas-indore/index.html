Summary

Chandrahas Joshi, 49, Senior Manager Quality Assurance at a mid-sized auto-component firm in Indore, has won his company's "Best Employee of the Year" award seven years running. His wife Anjali designed a glass-fronted, internally lit mahogany cabinet for the trophies; she dusts it every Sunday with a separate cloth, twenty minutes precisely. Last Tuesday, home alone with the family scattered, he sat in front of the cabinet and watched himself for forty minutes by his watch. Nothing arrived. No pride, no gratitude, not even emptiness — just the texture of a stranger's possessions. He noticed a streak of dust Anjali had missed and walked to the kitchen. He has not slept properly since. He cannot tell his boss (will be read as burnout), his wife (her love is in that cabinet), or his daughter (in her own first-year storm). Both Virus and Chatur recognise the moment instantly because they have lived it themselves and never said so out loud. Chatur opens — the 1996 ICE convocation speech he memorised over nine days, the trophy received, and two days he could not get out of bed afterwards. Virus matches it — a night in the Director's office at ICE when an NIRF rank movement that should have meant everything meant nothing, and he sat for forty minutes alone. Three men, three rooms, one cabinet. The diagnosis is not pathology. It is the most honest x-ray Chandrahas has ever taken of his own life. The trophy cabinet was never satisfaction; it was a machine of "next" that finally stalled. The cure is not therapy or rest. The advice is structured. Keep the job — the daughter is in first year, the boy is fourteen, financial duty is real. But this year, set a private target: lose the eighth award. Let a younger Six Sigma champion win. Clap from the floor and feel relief, not loss; the eighth trophy is what would imprison him for five more years, and its absence is graduation. Sit Anjali down this weekend, say one careful sentence — that the cabinet has shifted meaning for him, that this is no comment on her love but a request for help, that they should build something new together. Start, in the drawing room, a tiny ITI mentorship — five young apprentices from Pithampur SEZ or Sanwer Road, two evenings a week, tea and a whiteboard, eighteen years of QA passed forward without a trophy attached. Anjali's twenty Sunday minutes of polishing will redirect into something living. Chatur closes by going further than the column ever does — admit he is in the same room, with bigger badges and the same emptiness, and that the most important first step is not the mentorship or the wife or the awards but telling one trusted human being this week that the forty-minute silence happened. Three men in three cities are sitting in front of the same cabinet. Speak so the silence is not solitary. The feeling is not the disease; it is the compass.
